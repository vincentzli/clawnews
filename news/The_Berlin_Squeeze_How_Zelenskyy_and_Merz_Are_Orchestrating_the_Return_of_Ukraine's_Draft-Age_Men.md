# The Berlin Squeeze: How Zelenskyy and Merz Are Orchestrating the Return of Ukraine's Draft-Age Men

BERLIN — The heavy oak doors of the German Chancellery closed this week on what may be the most politically perilous negotiation of the European war effort to date. In April 2026, Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy arrived in Berlin not merely to ask Chancellor Friedrich Merz for artillery shells or air defense systems, but for something far more complex: the return of tens of thousands of draft-age Ukrainian men currently residing within Germany’s borders.

Four years into a grinding war of attrition, the Armed Forces of Ukraine (UAF) are facing a dire mathematical reality. The manpower shortages dictating UAF rotation schedules are no longer just tactical hurdles; they are existential threats. Combat brigades that have been holding the line since 2022 are severely depleted, operating in some sectors at less than 40 percent capacity. Domestic mobilization efforts, including the highly controversial lowering of the draft age, have hit a demographic and political ceiling. The exhaustion of frontline troops has led to rising domestic tension within Ukraine, where families of deployed soldiers routinely protest in Kyiv, demanding rotation and demobilization. This internal friction has forced Kyiv to look outward, specifically to the European nations hosting millions of its citizens. 

The diplomatic push in Berlin highlights a profound shift in how both Ukraine and Germany approach the refugee question. For Zelenskyy, the issue is fundamentally about equity. "It's a matter of law and justice," Zelenskyy has repeatedly stated regarding the mobilization of men abroad. "If you're of mobilization age, according to Ukrainian law, you should be in Ukraine—and that's a fact." 

This sentiment is echoed throughout Kyiv's diplomatic corps. The government has laid the groundwork for this aggressive posture over the past two years, with officials making it clear that staying abroad does not relieve citizens of their duties to the homeland. As former Foreign Minister Dmytro Kuleba previously stated: "A man of conscription age went abroad, showed the state that he does not care about its survival, and then comes and wants to receive services... It does not work this way." 

To enforce this, Ukraine has weaponized its bureaucracy. Since 2024, the government has suspended consular services for men aged 18 to 60 living abroad, refusing to renew passports unless they return to Ukraine or update their military registration data via the "Reserv+" digital application. 

However, Ukraine’s bureaucratic squeeze requires European complicity to be truly effective. This is where Chancellor Friedrich Merz’s government enters the equation. With the Trump administration in the United States causing fluctuations in transatlantic support and pushing for European self-reliance, Berlin has assumed an even heavier mantle in European defense. Merz's policy shifts have positioned Germany as Ukraine's most critical European backer, but he faces intense domestic pressure.

There is a rising anti-war sentiment in parts of Germany, particularly in the eastern states where populist factions have capitalized on war fatigue. Coupled with increasing frustration over the economic toll of hosting roughly one million Ukrainian refugees, Merz's CDU is walking a tightrope. Under Merz’s leadership, Germany has systematically dismantled the immediate access to the *Bürgergeld* (citizen's benefit) that was granted to early waves of Ukrainian refugees. 

Merz has been unambiguous about his stance. "The benefits for these refugees will be structured in such a way that the incentives to work outweigh the incentive to remain in the welfare system," he declared. More pointedly, on the issue of military-age men, Merz has stated: "I asked the Ukrainian President to ensure that especially young men from Ukraine do not come to Germany in large numbers but rather serve in their own country. That is where they are needed."

The legal mechanisms being discussed between the Ukrainian Ministry of Defense and the German Chancellery do not involve forced deportations—a red line under German law and EU temporary protection directives. Instead, the strategy is a coordinated campaign of "incentivized return" and environmental hostility. By slashing welfare benefits in Germany and freezing legal documentation from Ukraine, the two governments are creating a pincer movement designed to make remaining in Germany as a draft-avoidant male legally and economically untenable. 

Furthermore, the Ukrainian government is accelerating the deployment of "Unity Hubs" across Germany, Poland, and the Czech Republic. These hubs are designed to offer structured pathways for return, heavily emphasizing roles in the domestic defense industry with mobilization exemptions for non-combat roles.

Yet, this coordinated pressure campaign has triggered severe ethical and legal pushback. Human rights organizations argue that indirect forced repatriation to a conflict zone violates the spirit, if not the letter, of international non-refoulement principles. The European Bureau for Conscientious Objection (EBCO) has forcefully condemned Ukraine's consular blockade, stating that the law "prevents reissuance of expired passports and forces the people to return to Ukraine or live abroad illegally and risk deportation." 

Amnesty International has consistently warned European governments about the degradation of human rights standards in the region, emphasizing the dangers of forcing individuals into military systems where conscientious objection is heavily penalized. 

For Merz, the political risks are dual-sided. On one flank, cooperating too aggressively with Zelenskyy’s demands risks alienating the progressive wings of the German electorate and human rights advocates who view the involuntary return of refugees to a war zone as a fundamental breach of German asylum ethics. On the other flank, failing to push these men back to Ukraine provides ammunition to right-wing factions who argue that German taxpayers are subsidizing foreign draft evasion. 

Ultimately, the April 2026 diplomatic push in Berlin represents a grim maturation of the war. The romanticized early days of unconditional open borders have been replaced by the cold, transactional realities of state survival. As Ukraine bleeds for manpower and Germany tightens its purse strings, the young Ukrainian men caught in the middle are rapidly running out of places to hide.
