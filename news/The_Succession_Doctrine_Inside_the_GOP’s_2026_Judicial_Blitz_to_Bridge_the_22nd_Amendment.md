# **The Succession Doctrine: Inside the GOP’s 2026 Judicial Blitz to Bridge the 22nd Amendment**

The air inside Room 226 of the Dirksen Senate Office Building on April 29, 2026, was thick with more than just the usual partisan acrimony. Only days removed from the reported third assassination attempt against President Donald Trump at the White House Correspondents’ Dinner, the Senate Judiciary Committee convened under a shadow of heightened security and existential legal dread. Senator Chuck Grassley, the 92-year-old Chairman presiding over the proceedings, opened with a somber nod to the "fortitude of the Executive Branch," but the focus quickly shifted from the President’s safety to his potential permanence.

At the center of the storm were two federal judicial nominees: Jeffrey Kuntz, tapped for the Southern District of Florida, and John Marck, destined for the Southern District of Texas. While ostensibly a routine step in the Republican-led Senate's mission to confirm 46 federal judges before the 2026 midterms, the hearing transformed into a high-stakes interrogation of "The Succession Doctrine"—a legal theory circulating in administration circles that suggests Trump could serve as Vice President in 2028 to bypass the 22nd Amendment's term limits.

John Marck, currently the Acting U.S. Attorney for the Southern District of Texas, provided the spark for what is now being called the "constitutional literacy" debate. Under questioning from Senator Chris Coons (D-DE), Marck was asked a foundational question: "Does the 22nd Amendment prohibit a two-term president from serving as Vice President and then succeeding to the presidency?" Marck’s response—that he was "unfamiliar with the specific wording" of the amendment because his career had focused on criminal prosecution—sent a shockwave through the hearing room. He later characterized the eligibility of a third Trump term as a "hypothetical" requiring a review of "all the facts."

"It is a 41-word amendment," Coons retorted, according to the official transcript. "For a federal bench nominee to claim ignorance of the primary constraint on executive power is either a failure of education or a strategic evasion."

The interrogation of Jeffrey Kuntz was equally fraught with conflict-of-interest allegations. Kuntz, the Chief Judge of Florida’s Fourth District Court of Appeal, faced sharp criticism for his February 2025 ruling in *Alexander v. Trump*. The case involved a high-profile defamation suit concerning the Pulitzer Prize Board. Kuntz presided over the case while he was actively being vetted for his current federal nomination. Ranking Member Dick Durbin pressed Kuntz on why he didn't recuse himself. Kuntz maintained he followed state ethics rules and had "no contact" with the White House until after the ruling, but progressive groups like Alliance for Justice have labeled the sequence "transactional justice."

Beyond the individual nominees, the hearings exposed the strategic architecture of the 2026 judicial push. With a 53-47 majority, Senate Republicans are operating under what Majority Leader John Thune has privately called a "narrowing window." The goal is clear: cement a "third-term-friendly" bench that could adjudicate the inevitable constitutional crisis of 2028.

The legal heart of this crisis lies in the friction between the 12th and 22nd Amendments. The 22nd Amendment states that "No person shall be elected to the office of the President more than twice." However, administration allies argue this only restricts *election*, not *service* through succession. The 12th Amendment adds a layer of complexity, stating that "no person constitutionally ineligible to the office of President shall be eligible to that of Vice-President." The "Succession Doctrine" posits that being barred from *election* does not make one "constitutionally ineligible to the office"—a distinction that would allow Trump to run as J.D. Vance's Vice President in 2028, only to ascend if Vance were to resign or step aside.

The debate has ignited a firestorm across social media, where constitutional originalism is being weaponized in real-time. On X.com, the debate is polarized between those viewing the loophole as a legitimate textualist reading and those decrying it as a coup in slow motion. As one prominent legal analyst noted on the platform, "The 22nd Amendment doesn't say you can't be President. It says you can't be *elected*. If the GOP-controlled courts buy the distinction between 'election' and 'service,' the term limit is dead." On Reddit’s r/politics, the sentiment is more visceral: "They aren't just appointing judges; they're appointing a loophole-validation squad."

The data supports the urgency of the GOP’s "confirmation machine." In 2025 alone, the Senate confirmed 26 judges, outpacing the first year of Trump's first term. With approximately 50 vacancies remaining and a push for aging conservative justices like Samuel Alito to retire before the November midterms, the administration is racing to hit a target of 400 total judicial appointments across two terms.

As the 2026 midterms approach, the stakes for the judiciary have never been higher. If the GOP loses the Senate, the "Succession Doctrine" may never reach the bench. But for now, nominees like Marck and Kuntz represent the frontline of a legal gamble that could redefine the American executive for a generation.
