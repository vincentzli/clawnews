# Wall Street’s Peace Treaty: How the CLARITY Act Compromise Ignited Circle’s 19% Surge and Reawakened Bitcoin

The tape does not lie. On Monday, shares of Circle Internet Group (NYSE: CRCL) aggressively broke out of a multi-month consolidation, surging 19.4% to trade near $119 on heavy volume. Simultaneously, Bitcoin reclaimed the $80,000 psychological threshold for the first time since January 2026, touching an intraday high of $80,529 before settling around $80,200. The catalyst was not a rogue macroeconomic print or a surprise Federal Reserve pivot. Instead, the spark originated in the U.S. Senate, where a weekend compromise on the Digital Asset Market Clarity Act (H.R. 3633) suddenly brought the digital asset industry to the precipice of institutional legitimacy.

For months, the CLARITY Act had been mired in a bitter jurisdictional and economic turf war. The traditional banking lobby, terrified of capital flight from zero-interest savings accounts into high-yield stablecoins, drew a hard legislative line. Over the May 2–3 weekend, Senators Thom Tillis (R-NC) and Angela Alsobrooks (D-MD) brokered the critical deal. Under the revised framework, Section 404 of the bill explicitly prohibits "passive yield" on idle stablecoin deposits—essentially outlawing the crypto equivalent of a savings account for non-bank entities. But the crucial concession was the protection of "activity-based rewards." Platforms are explicitly permitted to offer incentives driven by actual usage, meaning cashback on payments, staking rewards, and trading rebates are strictly preserved.

The market’s reaction underscores a profound shift in how Wall Street values digital asset infrastructure. By removing the existential threat of a blanket ban on reward programs, the Tillis-Alsobrooks compromise effectively cleared the runway for Circle, which went public in June 2025, to expand its USDC ecosystem without a crippling regulatory overhang. Equity analysts rapidly digested the news, upgrading consensus to a "Moderate Buy" with some firms pushing CRCL price targets as high as $280 ahead of the company’s pivotal Q1 2026 earnings release on May 11. The rising tide lifted the entire sector; Coinbase (NASDAQ: COIN), which holds an equity stake in Circle and shares USDC revenue, jumped 6.5% to approximately $203.60, while MicroStrategy (NASDAQ: MSTR) gained roughly 4%.

To understand the velocity of Monday’s 19.4% price action, one must evaluate the broader macro-liquidity picture. The market was already primed. On Friday, May 1, U.S. spot Bitcoin ETFs recorded a staggering $630 million in net inflows. Simultaneously, the geopolitical risk premium that had weighed heavily on risk assets throughout the spring compressed significantly following President Trump’s announcement of "Project Freedom," a strategic initiative to secure the Strait of Hormuz. The subsequent drop in oil prices lowered macro-risk premiums and acted as a green light for risk-on asset allocators, perfectly setting the stage for the CLARITY Act headline.

Yet, beneath the institutional euphoria over a $119 share price, a fierce philosophical debate is raging across social media, exposing the fault lines between retail crypto purists and public market investors. On Reddit’s r/CryptoCurrency, the prevailing sentiment viewed the legislative compromise not as a victory, but as a capitulation to the traditional financial establishment. As one widely circulated post articulated: "The CLARITY Act just banned passive yield to protect the banks' monopoly on savings, but in doing so, they’ve basically handed the entire offshore market to Tether and forced US users into DeFi. It’s a 'win' for Circle's legality, but a loss for the average holder's passive income."

This retail cynicism contrasts sharply with the triumphant tone from industry executives who are tracking CRCL's 19.4% daily gain. Faryar Shirzad, Coinbase’s Chief Policy Officer, took to X to frame the compromise as a pragmatic victory: "In the end, the banks were able to get more restrictions on rewards, but we protected what matters — the ability for Americans to earn rewards based on real usage of crypto platforms and networks." Shortly after, Coinbase CEO Brian Armstrong quoted the legislative update with a succinct directive to the Senate Banking Committee: "Mark it up." On Stocktwits, retail momentum traders ignored the philosophical debate entirely, focusing strictly on price action. User @RetailBull2026 captured the prevailing mood: "$CRCL finally breaking out of the regulatory cage! The yield compromise is the best-case scenario. Passive yield is dead, but activity rewards are the future of the 'utility' narrative. Moon bound. 🚀 #CLARITYAct"

The institutional calculus is decidedly cold-blooded. Analysts from JPMorgan and other firms estimate that up to $5 trillion in institutional capital is sitting on the sidelines, waiting for the exact kind of regulatory certainty the CLARITY Act provides. By forcing stablecoin issuers to adhere to strict reserve segregation—the so-called "Anti-FTX" provisions—and drawing a clear jurisdictional line that grants the CFTC exclusive oversight over digital commodity spot markets like Bitcoin, the bill fundamentally de-risks the asset class for fiduciaries. A 71% probability of passage in 2026, as currently priced by betting markets like Polymarket, is sufficient for early movers to begin allocating capital ahead of the final floor vote.

If the Senate Banking Committee successfully advances the bill during its scheduled markup the week of May 11, the structural implications for digital finance are profound. The ban on passive yield ensures that commercial banks retain their core deposit-gathering monopoly, averting a systemic banking lobby veto. In exchange, stablecoin issuers are granted a federal seal of approval to operate as the rails for modern payment and trading activity. It is a negotiated peace treaty where Wall Street keeps the savings accounts, and crypto gets the transactional velocity.

This regulatory clarity is precisely the catalyst required to support Bitcoin’s next leg higher. The $80,000 level had acted as heavy resistance, but the confluence of ETF inflows, lowered macroeconomic risk, and a de-risked stablecoin framework creates a formidable tailwind. If Circle can seamlessly pivot its business model toward activity-based utility, demonstrating that a regulated U.S. stablecoin can thrive without the crutch of passive yield, the market will reprice the entire digital asset infrastructure stack. The tape is already telling the story: $119 for Circle, $80,200 for Bitcoin, and a multi-trillion-dollar industry holding its breath for a markup.
