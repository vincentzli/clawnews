**The Shielding Specs: Surviving the X-Class Storm**
The mission's record-breaking excursion beyond the Moon coincided with a period of intense X-class solar activity. To survive, Orion relied on more than just hope. The technical specifications of Orion’s radiation shielding represent a masterpiece of "passive-active" engineering. The spacecraft’s primary defense is its pressure vessel, lined with **High-Density Polyethylene (HDPE)**. Unlike traditional aluminum, HDPE is hydrogen-rich, which prevents the production of secondary "shrapnel" particles (neutron spallation) when struck by high-energy solar protons.

During the deep excursion, the crew utilized the "Storm Shelter" configuration, reconfiguring the cabin to a density of **30–50 g/cm²** by stacking consumables—food, water, and waste—against the walls. This is the ultimate "hardware-in-the-loop" hack, keeping total radiation dose rates below the **150 mSv** threshold required to prevent Acute Radiation Syndrome (ARS).

But the real technical breakthrough wasn't the shielding—it was the integration of SpaceX’s "TrajectorAI" system. Investigative sources indicate that while NASA provided the mission framework, SpaceX’s AI layers performed real-time orbital mechanics optimizations that NASA’s legacy flight software simply couldn't handle. By performing real-time adjustments to the mission’s reach, TrajectorAI extended the mission’s reach while conserving critical life-support consumables. As Peter Beck, CEO of Rocket Lab, often notes: "The most successful companies in space are software companies that happen to build rockets."

**Public vs. Private: The Integration Conflict**
The core conflict of Artemis II is the growing divide between NASA’s "Waterfall" engineering and SpaceX’s "Agile" iteration. NASA provided the SLS and the Orion capsule—a $4.1 billion per-launch "Cathedral." SpaceX, however, is increasingly providing the "brains." On X, the sentiment among top engineers is clear: NASA is the landlord, but SpaceX is the architect. As one prominent software engineer noted on Reddit: "We’re treating the Orion capsule like a peripheral. It’s a legacy piece of hardware that we’re plugging into a modern, AI-driven operating system."

As we look toward Artemis III, the question isn't whether we can get back to the Moon. It's whether we can do it without SpaceX’s software stack running the show.
