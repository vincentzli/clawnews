# "I Messed Up": NTSB Investigates Combined ATC Position and N90 Staffing in Fatal LaGuardia Collision

At 11:38 p.m. on Sunday, March 22, 2026, amid heavy rain and severely degraded visibility, Air Canada Express Flight 8646, a Bombardier CRJ900 operated by Jazz Aviation arriving from Montréal–Trudeau International Airport, was on its final landing rollout on LaGuardia Airport's Runway 4. Simultaneously, a Port Authority Aircraft Rescue and Firefighting (ARFF) vehicle—designated Truck 1—was crossing the active runway at Taxiway Delta. 

The impact destroyed the aircraft's nose and forward galley sections, killing both the 54-year-old captain and the 32-year-old first officer. According to early responder reports, one flight attendant was thrown from the aircraft upon impact, landing on the tarmac, but survived with serious injuries. The heavy ARFF vehicle, weighing over 80,000 pounds, was struck with such immense kinetic force that it overturned. In total, 41 individuals were transported to local hospitals. This included 39 passengers and crew from the regional jet, alongside two Port Authority officers who were trapped inside the overturned fire truck. By Monday afternoon, nine individuals remained under intensive hospital care.

The immediate sequence of events was captured on LiveATC.net recordings that circulated across platforms like X and Reddit. The incident began minutes earlier with an unrelated emergency. A United Airlines Boeing 737 MAX 8, operating as Flight 1442, had aborted its takeoff roll on Runway 13 due to reports of a "foul odor" and potential electrical smoke in the cabin. The lone on-duty air traffic controller managing the local sector diverted Truck 1 to respond to the United aircraft. In the process of navigating LaGuardia's taxiway network, the controller cleared Truck 1 to cross Runway 4. 

Seconds later, realizing the intersecting paths of the CRJ900 and the fire truck, the controller is heard urgently shouting, "Stop, Truck 1! Stop!" just moments before the impact. Following the collision, as alarms blared in the background of the frequency, the controller keyed the microphone and stated, "I messed up."

On social media, the aviation community analyzed the event through leaked audio and ground-level footage. In the r/aviation subreddit, eyewitness accounts from other pilots waiting on the tarmac emerged. Users analyzed telemetry data shared on the forum, indicating that the flight crew initiated maximum manual braking and reverse thrust in their final seconds. As one user noted, "If they hadn't stood on the brakes when they saw the truck emerge from the rain, the kinetic energy would have sheared the wings off. They saved the lives of the 72 passengers on board."

The NTSB's preliminary investigation, launched within hours of the crash, is pointing toward a systemic crisis at the New York Terminal Radar Approach Control (N90) facility. For years, the facility has been severely understaffed. As of early 2026, N90 is operating at approximately 58% to 60% of its targeted staffing level, with only 135 Certified Professional Controllers against a congressionally mandated target of 226. To cover the schedule gaps, controllers are subjected to mandatory six-day work weeks and 10-hour shifts, leading the National Air Traffic Controllers Association (NATCA) to warn of "cognitive overload" and chronic fatigue.

The controller involved in Sunday's collision was working a "combined position"—handling both ground movement and local air traffic control simultaneously. This practice is standard during late-night hours with lower traffic volume, but union officials argue it removes a critical layer of redundancy. This operational configuration was previously cited by the NTSB as a primary factor in the January 2025 midair collision near Reagan National Airport (DCA), which claimed 67 lives when a single controller became overwhelmed. The FAA, currently operating under the Trump administration, is managing the fallout of N90's severe shortages. A prior July 2024 initiative under the previous administration to relocate the Newark (EWR) sector from N90 to the Philadelphia TRACON (PHL) was intended to relieve pressure but was immediately plagued by radar outages, equipment failures, and reports of "workplace trauma" among relocated controllers.

Compounding the human fatigue is a technological lag in surface safety systems. LaGuardia is equipped with a standard Surface Movement Guidance and Control System (SMGCS), utilizing embedded runway lights and sensors designed for low-visibility operations below 1,200 feet Runway Visual Range (RVR). However, investigators are scrutinizing the uneven adoption of Advanced SMGCS (A-SMGCS) across ground vehicle fleets. A-SMGCS uses multilateration and surface radar to track transponder-equipped ground vehicles, providing air traffic controllers with automated, predictive conflict alerts.

While commercial aircraft are integrated into these warning systems, many older Port Authority utility and ARFF vehicles lack the necessary integrated transponders. LaGuardia’s uniquely compact, 680-acre footprint requires ground vehicles to cross active runways far more frequently than at modern, sprawling hub airports like Denver or Dallas-Fort Worth. In heavy rain, relying solely on human verbal clearance without real-time predictive warnings proved to be a critical vulnerability. As one X user pointed out in the aftermath: "The FAA has billions in funding, yet we are still relying on visual clearance in heavy rain at LGA? Unacceptable."

LaGuardia gradually reopened Monday afternoon after a nearly 15-hour closure that disrupted thousands of flights nationwide. The NTSB's final report is expected to take upwards of 18 months to complete. Investigators have indicated their focus will remain centered on the controller's workload, the delayed implementation of A-SMGCS technology in ground fleets, and the broader staffing mandates enforced at the N90 facility.
