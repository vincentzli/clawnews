# The Gasoline Mirage: Why Canada’s 2.4% Inflation Print is a "Less Bad" Omen for Macklem

In the world of central banking, a 21.2% monthly surge in any major commodity is usually grounds for a localized panic. Yet, as the March Consumer Price Index (CPI) hit the tape at 2.4% year-over-year, the mood at 234 Wellington Street was one of cautious relief rather than alarm. For Governor Tiff Macklem and the Bank of Canada (BoC), the latest data represents a profound divergence: a geopolitical "Iran war shock" is causing havoc at the pump, while the structural engine of Canadian domestic inflation is quietly cooling to levels not seen since the pre-pandemic era.

The headline figure of 2.4%—a jump from February’s 1.8%—initially looks like a setback. It wasn’t. Markets had braced for a print as high as 2.6%, factoring in the catastrophic closure of the Strait of Hormuz and the subsequent 21.2% spike in monthly gasoline prices, the largest on record. That the headline missed the 2.5% consensus despite a double-digit energy surge suggests that the "rest" of the Canadian economy is essentially flatlining.

"Apart from those fuel-related inflation spikes, the report was fairly benign," noted Leslie Preston, Managing Director and Senior Economist at TD Bank. The "benign" label is backed by the hard numbers. When gasoline is stripped out, Canada’s annual inflation rate actually decelerated to 2.2% in March, down from 2.4% in February. More importantly, the BoC’s preferred gauges of underlying pressure—CPI-median and CPI-trim—showed remarkable resilience. CPI-median held steady at 2.3%, while CPI-trim edged lower to 2.2%. In a 2026 landscape defined by President Trump’s aggressive trade posture and the resulting volatility in the USD/CAD pair, these core metrics provide the BoC with the "inflationary cover" it needs to decouple from a more hawkish Federal Reserve.

On Fintwit and Reddit’s r/CanadianInvestor, the debate has been sharp. Real-estate bulls were quick to point to the cooling shelter component, which dropped to 1.7% in March—a staggering decline from the 6-7% levels that haunted the 2023-2024 period. One widely shared post from @StephenPunwasi highlighted the tension: "The headline is a lie. If you aren't a commuter, your personal inflation is likely under 2%. The BoC is looking at a stagnant economy masked by an oil war." Another user on StockTwits argued the contrarian view: "Energy shocks are never 'one-offs.' If gas stays this high, it’s going to bleed into grocery logistics and services. Macklem can’t cut into a 21% gas spike without the Loonie tanking to 68 cents."

The "Loonie risk" is the ghost in the machine. With the overnight rate currently sitting at 2.25%, the BoC is already operating in a tighter environment than many expected for early 2026. However, the divergence with the U.S. is widening. While Fed Chair Jerome Powell grapples with "Trump-flation"—the inflationary tailwinds of universal 10% tariffs and massive infrastructure spending south of the border—Canada is dealing with a classic supply-side shock. 

The political context in Ottawa has also shifted the math. Following the 2025 Federal Election, the new Pierre Poilievre administration’s decision to eliminate the consumer carbon tax effective April 2025 has altered the year-over-year base effects. While the Industrial Carbon Tax rose to $110 per tonne on April 1, 2026, the absence of the consumer levy at the pump has simplified the CPI basket. Nevertheless, the March report was the final month distorted by the 2025 GST/HST tax holiday—a temporary measure that many analysts believe artificially suppressed prices last year. 

"The March data is the last of the 'noise' from the previous government's tax holidays," said Doug Porter, Chief Economist at BMO Capital Markets. "April will be our first 'clean' year-over-year look at the true state of the Canadian consumer."

The investigative reality is that the 21.2% gasoline surge is a localized fever, not a systemic infection. The Strait of Hormuz closure has removed nearly a fifth of global oil supply, but the Canadian consumer is already tapped out. Retail sales volumes were down 0.4% in February, and the unemployment rate has ticked up to 6.2%. There is simply no "excess demand" left to absorb higher energy costs and pass them through to other sectors. This is why BMO characterized the report as "two steps forward, one step back"—the energy shock is the "back," but the 1.7% shelter print and the 2.2% ex-gasoline figure are the "forward."

As of today, April 20, 2026, the federal government has launched a 10-cent-per-litre "excise tax holiday" to provide immediate relief at the pump. This move, while popular with voters, will likely further complicate the BoC’s data-gathering for the next three months. Traders are currently pricing in a 90%+ probability that Macklem will hold the 2.25% rate at the April 29 meeting. 

The real danger isn't that inflation re-accelerates; it’s that the BoC waits too long to recognize that the "gasoline mirage" is masking a deepening domestic recession. For investors, the play isn't on the headline 2.4%, but on the widening gap between a war-driven energy spike and a consumer base that is increasingly unable to pay the bill. If core inflation continues its sub-2.5% trajectory while the geopolitical crisis lingers, Macklem may be forced to cut rates into a rising oil market—a move that would test the very foundations of the Bank's credibility.
