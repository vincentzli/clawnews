# The EdTech Lazarus: Inside Skillsoft’s $1.26 EPS Pivot and the Great AI Re-Rating of 2026

On the morning of April 7, 2026, the screens at the New York Stock Exchange blinked with a set of numbers that defied the prevailing "EdTech is dead" narrative of the mid-2020s. Skillsoft Corp. (NYSE: SKIL) reported a fourth-quarter non-GAAP earnings per share of $1.26, a figure that technically missed consensus estimates by a single cent but triggered an explosive 22.4% rally, sending shares to a two-year high of $5.35 by the closing bell. For a company that had lost over 70% of its market capitalization since 2024, the reaction was more than a relief rally; it was a fundamental re-rating of what the market is willing to pay for legacy educational assets in an "AI-native" economy.

The tension in the Skillsoft story lies in the delta between its legacy weight and its futuristic aspirations. While the top-line revenue of $130.7 million represented a modest 2.3% year-over-year contraction, the internal plumbing of the business has been entirely re-engineered. Management revealed that usage of CAISY™, the company’s AI-powered conversation simulator, had surged 146% year-over-year. More tellingly, AI skill benchmark completions within its Percipio platform skyrocketed by 994% in the fiscal year ending January 31, 2026. This isn't just a content library anymore; it is a high-frequency skills-verification engine.

The market’s aggressive response to the $1.26 print is rooted in a brutal but necessary structural clean-up. For years, Skillsoft’s performance was dragged down by its Global Knowledge (GK) segment—a capital-intensive, instructor-led training business that felt increasingly anachronistic in a world of generative AI. In today’s earnings call, CFO John Frederick essentially confirmed the segment's death sentence, noting that a "partnership-driven model is more appropriate than continued ownership." By issuing fiscal 2027 revenue guidance of $400 million to $410 million exclusively for its digital Talent Development Solutions (TDS) segment, Skillsoft effectively signaled it is ready to excise the GK tumor.

"Skillsoft is a broken stock but a fixing business," noted one widely-shared post on Fintwit today. "The market is finally realizing that enterprise training isn't about the videos—it’s about the orchestration layer that guarantees job readiness." Indeed, the valuation gap has become impossible to ignore. Even after today’s surge, SKIL trades at a trailing price-to-earnings multiple of roughly 0.95x, a staggering discount to the broader software-as-a-service (SaaS) sector. Contrarians on Reddit’s r/ValueInvesting have spent the last week debating whether this is a "deep value play" or a "liquidity trap," citing the company’s current ratio of 0.84 as a red flag for short-term debt coverage.

However, the "short-term" bears may have been caught in a classic squeeze. Short interest in SKIL stood at 13.1% of the float heading into the print, with more than eight days to cover. As the Adjusted EBITDA margin expanded to 24%—up from 22% a year ago—and quarterly free cash flow doubled to $27 million, the "liquidity crisis" narrative began to crumble. Skillsoft’s debt maturity profile is largely back-ended toward 2028 and 2029, and while the company pays a punishing $60 million in annual interest, its interest coverage ratio has stabilized at 1.2x.

The macro backdrop of 2026 has provided an unexpected tailwind. The Trump-Vance administration’s focus on domestic vocational reskilling and "American-made human capital" has put a premium on verifiable technical certifications. As the administration pushes for more efficient paths to high-paying jobs without four-year degrees, Skillsoft’s pivot toward "skills intelligence" aligns perfectly with the federal policy shift. The recent merger between Coursera and Udemy—a consolidation play aimed at creating a $1.5 billion revenue behemoth—has further validated the "outcome-based" model that Skillsoft is pursuing.

Yet, skepticism remains. "Everyone screaming 'value play' on $SKIL needs to look at the current ratio," cautioned a prominent voice on StockTwits under the handle @MacroBear26. "They can't cover short-term debt and the technicals are in a death spiral. It’s not a squeeze, it’s a liquidation." The tension between these views is the heartbeat of the 2026 EdTech trade. If Skillsoft can successfully divest the GK segment and maintain its 99% dollar retention rate in the TDS segment, the $1.26 EPS print will be remembered as the moment the market realized that legacy data, when paired with agentic AI, is a formidable barrier to entry.

The real investigative question for 2027 is whether the "AI-native" label is a genuine transformation or a survivalist’s re-branding. Skillsoft now uses AI for over 50% of its content production, a move that has gutted its operating expenses but raises questions about the long-term value of the content itself. If AI can build the courses, can’t AI also replace the need for them? For now, the market has decided that the "human + AI" era needs a trainer, and Skillsoft, with its $1.26 profit and $27 million in new cash, is the first legacy player to prove it can keep the lights on during the transition.
