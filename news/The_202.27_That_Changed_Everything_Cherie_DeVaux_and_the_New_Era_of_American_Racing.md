# **The 2:02.27 That Changed Everything: Cherie DeVaux and the New Era of American Racing**

At 6:58 p.m. on Saturday, May 2, 2026, the roar of 150,415 spectators at Churchill Downs underwent a sudden, dissonant shift. As the field for the 152nd Kentucky Derby turned for home, the pre-race narrative—centered on Todd Pletcher’s heavily favored Renegade—was being systematically dismantled by a 23-1 long shot and a trainer who had spent 22 years climbing the backstretch hierarchy. When Golden Tempo thrust his nose across the wire to win by a neck, the official time of 2:02.27 did more than just mark the fastest Derby in three years; it signaled the definitive end of the sport’s most enduring gender barrier.

Cherie DeVaux’s victory as the first female trainer to win the "Run for the Roses" was not a product of luck, but of a calculated, high-stakes tactical pivot. Entering the spring, Golden Tempo, a Phipps Stable and St. Elias Stable homebred by Hall of Famer Curlin out of the Bernardini mare Carrumba, was regarded as a "lazy" closer with a frustrating ceiling. Following a lackluster third in the Louisiana Derby, DeVaux made the executive decision to add blinkers—a move often seen as a last resort for unmotivated three-year-olds. 

"We had to have faith in the process," DeVaux said in the post-race press conference, her voice steady despite the weight of the moment. "He’s a dead closer, and we knew if he had the extra ground, he was going to make it. We aren't here for our egos; we’re here for the horse."

The investigative depth of this victory reveals a week of intense medical management. Behind the scenes, DeVaux was battling a minor case of "scratches"—cracked heels—on Golden Tempo’s hind legs. While the industry buzzed about the 5-1 favorite Renegade, DeVaux was personally applying Vaseline to the colt’s heels before morning gallops to shield them from the Churchill dirt, ensuring the condition remained a non-factor. It was the kind of meticulous, hands-on horsemanship honed during her years as a top assistant to Chad Brown and her early days as an exercise rider.

The tactical execution fell to jockey José Ortiz, who achieved the rare "Oaks-Derby sweep" after winning the Kentucky Oaks the previous day aboard Always a Runner. Ortiz’s ride was a masterclass in patience. Positioned 18th in a field of 20 for the first three-quarters of a mile, he ignored the blistering early fractions of :22.68 and :46.44 set by Six Speed. While the front-runners "hit a wall" in the final furlong, Ortiz moved Golden Tempo into a 13th-place position at the top of the stretch before unleashing a "symphonic" late kick.

The final hundred yards produced a dramatic brother-versus-brother duel. Irad Ortiz Jr., riding Renegade, had struck the front 50 yards from the wire. For a fleeting second, the Pletcher-Ortiz Jr. powerhouse seemed destined for another trophy. But Golden Tempo found an extra gear, surging past the favorite in the shadow of the wire. "Hats off to the winner," Irad Ortiz Jr. remarked afterward. "They ran a huge race."

The victory resonates far beyond the $3.1 million winner's share of the $5 million purse. In an industry where female trainers have historically struggled to secure the high-pedigree "Saturday afternoon horses" owned by the sport's elite syndicates, DeVaux’s triumph with a Phipps-bred colt is a structural shift. Daisy Phipps, representing the legendary racing family, called DeVaux a "phenomenal horsewoman," emphasizing that gender was never the metric for their partnership—performance was.

The social media response was immediate and bifurcated. On X.com, the racing community erupted, with one prominent analyst noting, "The 'can a woman win it' question is officially dead. DeVaux didn't just win; she out-trained the best in the world with a 23-1 shot." On Reddit’s r/horseracing, users highlighted the tactical brilliance of the blinkers, with one post gaining traction for detailing the "DeVaux-Ortiz masterclass in pace analysis."

As the Triple Crown season shifts to the Preakness Stakes, the political and social backdrop of 2026 remains a factor. Under the second administration of President Donald Trump, the horse racing industry has faced renewed scrutiny over federal oversight and safety standards. DeVaux’s win, characterized by transparency and horse-first ethics, provides the industry with a timely, positive face for the mid-2020s.

For DeVaux, who nearly left the sport at a crossroads in 2017 before her husband, bloodstock agent David Ingordo, urged her to "give it three years," the victory is a vindication of a two-decade climb. "I’m just glad I don’t have to answer that question anymore," she joked, referring to the perennial inquiry about when a woman would finally break through. With the 152nd Derby in the books, the focus now turns to whether Golden Tempo can maintain this "rocket" surge in Baltimore. For now, the dirt at Churchill Downs belongs to Cherie DeVaux.
