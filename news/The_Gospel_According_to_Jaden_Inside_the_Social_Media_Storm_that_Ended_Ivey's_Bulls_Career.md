# The Gospel According to Jaden: Inside the Social Media Storm that Ended Ivey's Bulls Career

At exactly 5:00 p.m. CT on Monday, March 30, 2026, the Chicago Bulls issued a one-sentence press release that reverberated far beyond the United Center’s hardwood. "The Chicago Bulls announced today that the team has waived guard Jaden Ivey due to conduct detrimental to the team." The statement, devoid of the usual "we wish him the best" platitudes, marked the abrupt end of a tenure that lasted just four games and 47 days. For Ivey, a former No. 5 overall pick with All-Star potential, the waiver was not about his box score or his lingering left knee injury; it was the climax of a three-week series of events that has forced the NBA into its most precarious cultural standoff in decades.

The timeline of Ivey's fallout began shortly after his February 2026 trade from the Detroit Pistons. Acquired for a package of young talent and draft capital, Ivey was viewed as the explosive backcourt partner the Bulls needed for a deep playoff run. However, after appearing in just four games, a recurring knee injury sidelined him. It was during this period of physical rehabilitation that Ivey’s social media presence shifted from standard athlete updates to a series of increasingly intense, often hour-long Instagram Live sessions filmed alone in his car.

The "religious rants," as they were swiftly dubbed by NBA media, reached a fever pitch in mid-March. In one session on March 22, which peaked at over 25,000 live viewers, Ivey delivered a sermon that directly challenged the league’s corporate identity. "The world proclaims LGBTQ, right? They proclaim Pride Month, and the NBA does too," Ivey stated, staring directly into his smartphone camera. "To celebrate unrighteousness is not of God. You can’t walk with the Lord and celebrate what He calls sin."

The backlash was instantaneous. For a league that has positioned itself as a bastion of progressive values, Ivey’s comments were a direct hit to the "public confidence" mentioned in Article 24 of the NBA Constitution. But Ivey did not stop at the league’s social initiatives. In a subsequent broadcast, he characterized Catholicism as a "false religion" that "does not lead to salvation." The remark was particularly jarring given that his mother, Niele Ivey, remains the head coach of the women’s basketball team at the University of Notre Dame—one of the world's most prominent Catholic institutions.

Inside the Bulls' front office, the situation was described by team sources as "unsustainable." Reports indicate that Vice President Artūras Karnišovas and General Manager Marc Eversley held multiple private meetings with Ivey, urging him to tone down the rhetoric to avoid "distracting from the team's playoff push." Instead, Ivey doubled down. When fans expressed concern for his mental health in the comments section, he responded with confrontational religious rhetoric. "God does not hear your Prayer if you are a sinner," he replied to one user. To another, he wrote: "You don't judge righteously because you yourself are not righteous."

The Bulls' decision to waive Ivey is an expensive move with long-term implications. Ivey is in the final year of his rookie contract, earning approximately $10.1 million for the 2025-26 season. By citing "conduct detrimental," the Bulls are effectively challenging the guaranteed nature of that salary, a move that is expected to trigger a grievance from the NBA Players Association (NBPA). While the NBPA has not issued a formal public statement as of Monday evening, sources suggest they are reviewing the contractual protections regarding religious expression and the definition of "detrimental" speech.

Commissioner Adam Silver finds himself caught in a complex regulatory environment. Throughout late 2025 and early 2026, Silver’s directives have focused heavily on "Competitive Integrity" and "Gambling," following a series of FBI investigations into prop betting. However, Silver’s tenure is also defined by a defense of LGBTQ+ rights, most notably his 2017 decision to move the All-Star Game from Charlotte. To ignore Ivey’s comments would be to abandon that precedent; to punish them is to risk a "free speech" revolt from a segment of the fanbase that views the league as over-politicized.

The political climate of 2026 has only amplified the conflict. With President Trump currently in the second year of his second term and former Vice President Kamala Harris leading private sector opposition, the national debate over corporate "conduct" codes has found a new proxy in Jaden Ivey. On platforms like X, conservative factions have rallied behind Ivey. "The NBA will fine you for missing a game but fire you for quoting the Bible," one prominent X user noted in a post that garnered 100,000 likes within hours of the waiver. Conversely, LGBTQ+ advocacy groups have characterized the Bulls' action as a necessary step against discriminatory rhetoric.

For Ivey, the future is a void. At 24 years old, his talent is undeniable, but the "conduct detrimental" tag is historically difficult to shed in a league that prioritizes brand stability. As the playoffs approach, the narrative should be about the Bulls' championship aspirations. Instead, the league is bracing for a legal and cultural battle that could redefine the boundaries of player expression in the modern era. In his final Instagram post before the waiver was announced, Ivey wrote: "I am nothing without Jesus. The truth is worth the cost."
