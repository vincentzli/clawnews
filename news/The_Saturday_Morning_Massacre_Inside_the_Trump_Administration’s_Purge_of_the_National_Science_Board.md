# The Saturday Morning Massacre: Inside the Trump Administration’s Purge of the National Science Board

At 8:15 A.M. on Saturday, April 25, 2026, the 24 members of the National Science Board (NSB) received a uniform, three-paragraph email from the White House Office of Presidential Personnel. The message was clinical: their services were no longer required, effective immediately. By noon, their access to the National Science Foundation (NSF) internal portals had been revoked. In a single stroke, President Trump had decapitated the governing body of the nation’s $9 billion primary science agency, ending a 76-year tradition of staggered, apolitical leadership that had survived every administration since Harry Truman signed the National Science Foundation Act into law in 1950.

This "Saturday Morning Massacre" is not merely a personnel shuffle; it is the culmination of a year-long campaign to dismantle the independence of federal scientific inquiry. Since taking office in January 2025, the administration has moved with "chainsaw" efficiency—a term frequently used by Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE) co-leads Elon Musk and Vivek Ramaswamy—to align the NSF with executive policy goals. The dismissal of the entire board, including Chair Dario Gil of IBM and Vice Chair Victor McCrary, removes the final institutional buffer between the White House and the peer-review process that dictates which American ideas receive funding.

The legal bridgehead for this move is a deliberate exploitation of a 1950 statutory loophole. While the NSF Act of 1950 explicitly states in 42 U.S.C. § 1864 that the Director serves "unless sooner removed by the President," it is famously silent on the removal of Board members. Historically, this silence was interpreted as a shield, ensuring that the 24 eminent scientists and engineers, serving six-year staggered terms, could provide continuity across political cycles. However, a White House legal memorandum circulated this morning argues that under the "Unitary Executive" theory, statutory silence equals at-will removal. The memo cites the 2021 precedent set by the Biden administration, which removed members of the Commission of Fine Arts without cause, as the floor for executive authority. "If the law doesn't say 'for cause,' they serve at the pleasure," a senior administration official told investigative reporters. "The era of the 'independent' fourth branch of government is over."

The purge follows a brutal 15-month period for the NSF. By mid-2025, DOGE "workforce-optimization" teams had already terminated over 1,600 active grants. These cancellations targeted research deemed "ideologically biased" or "fiscally wasteful," including a $1.5 million project on "embodied learning" and thousands of grants involving Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI) metrics. The administration’s FY2026 "skinny budget" proposes a staggering 55% cut to the NSF’s total funding, dropping it from $8.8 billion to $3.9 billion. This fiscal contraction is paired with a new 15% cap on indirect cost reimbursements—the "overhead" that universities rely on to maintain labs. For major research institutions like MIT and Stanford, which typically negotiate rates between 50% and 60%, the 15% cap represents a potential bankruptcy-level event for their research wings.

The timing of the NSB dismissal is inextricably linked to the "America’s AI Action Plan" and the escalating quantum computing race with China. David Sacks, who transitioned from "AI Czar" to Co-Chair of the President’s Council of Advisors on Science and Technology (PCAST) in March 2026, has been a vocal critic of "curiosity-driven" basic research. In a March 2026 framework, Sacks argued that federal science must shift toward "applied metascience" to support "Project Stargate"—a multibillion-dollar federal initiative to build AI data centers. The administration views the traditional NSB as a relic of a "slow-growth" academic culture that prioritized peer-reviewed papers over geopolitical dominance. "We are in a Manhattan Project moment for AI," one PCAST advisor noted on X.com this morning. "We don't need a board that debates the social implications of algorithms; we need a board that ensures we have the compute to win."

The reaction from the scientific community has been a mixture of panic and strategic defiance. Barbara Snyder, President of the Association of American Universities (AAU), issued a rare weekend statement calling the mass dismissal an "unprecedented assault on the integrity of the American research enterprise." Snyder warned that by politicizing the NSB, the administration is "guaranteeing that the best scientific minds will abandon federal service, leaving a vacuum that our global rivals are eager to fill." On Reddit’s r/politics, the move was described by one prominent user as "the final nail in the coffin for objective science in the US," while supporters in r/conservative argued it was a necessary "drain the swamp" move for a "bloated and woke" NSF.

Representative Zoe Lofgren, Ranking Member of the House Science Committee, was more blunt. "This isn't a reorganization; it’s a capture," she said in a televised interview. "They are clearing the decks to install loyalists who will rubber-stamp the defunding of climate science and the redirection of basic research funds into private-sector AI contracts." Lofgren pointed to potential candidates for the "New NSB" currently circulating in White House circles, which include Silicon Valley venture capitalists and industry executives like Marc Andreessen and former U.S. CTO Michael Kratsios, rather than the traditional mix of university presidents and Nobel laureates.

As the 250th anniversary of the Declaration of Independence approaches in July—the self-imposed sunset date for DOGE—the administration appears determined to leave behind a radically different federal landscape. The NSF, once a beacon of meritocratic, independent inquiry, is being refitted as an engine of "Energy and AI Dominance." For the 24 scientists who lost their positions this morning, the message is clear: in the new Washington, "independence" is a luxury the administration believes the nation can no longer afford.
