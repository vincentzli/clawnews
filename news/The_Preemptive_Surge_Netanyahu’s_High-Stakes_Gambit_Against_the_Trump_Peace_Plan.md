# **The Preemptive Surge: Netanyahu’s High-Stakes Gambit Against the Trump Peace Plan**

The smoke rising from the Isfahan University of Technology and the solid-fuel missile facilities in Parchin this week did more than signal a tactical expansion of Operation Roaring Lion; it signaled a Prime Minister in a terminal struggle for political survival. On Tuesday, March 24, 2026, Benjamin Netanyahu issued a directive for a "48-hour surge" against Iranian industrial and military infrastructure—a move that senior defense officials in Tel Aviv and Washington describe as a calculated geopolitical maneuver to subvert President Donald Trump’s looming "Comprehensive Plan to End the Gaza Conflict."

For Netanyahu, the clock is no longer measured in electoral cycles, but in the hours remaining before a U.S.-brokered ceasefire takes effect. Israeli intelligence reportedly obtained a copy of the Trump administration's 15-point framework, which proposes lifting nuclear sanctions on Tehran in exchange for a total cessation of hostilities and the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz. Netanyahu’s response was a flurry of kinetic activity: hitting over 1,000 specific weapons production sites, including Iran’s only facility for submarine development and factories casting warheads in Tehran. By maximizing damage now, Netanyahu aims to render any diplomatic "freeze" irrelevant, ensuring Iran’s arms industry is degraded for a generation regardless of the final terms negotiated in Washington.

Yet, while the IDF strikes abroad, the Prime Minister’s domestic foundations are liquefying. The resignation of Ziv Agmon, the acting chief of staff and long-time loyalist, on Wednesday, March 25, has stripped away the final layer of Netanyahu’s inner circle. The "Baboon Recordings"—a series of leaked private messages revealed by Channel 12—exposed Agmon using derogatory racial slurs against Mizrahi Jews and Moroccan-origin members of the Likud party, referring to them as "monkeys" and "baboons." More damagingly, Agmon was caught stating that Netanyahu "has to go home" following the security failures of the previous years. The scandal has not only alienated the Likud’s core base but has triggered a mass exodus of political support that once seemed impenetrable.

In the streets of Tel Aviv and Jerusalem, the 48-hour strike has failed to trigger the "rally around the flag" effect the Prime Minister’s Office anticipated. Instead, it has ignited the largest nationwide protests since the start of the Trump presidency. Tens of thousands are calling for immediate early elections, fueled by polling data that suggests a permanent loss of the Prime Minister’s majority. The latest surveys from Zman Yisrael place Likud at 28 seats, while the "anti-Netanyahu bloc"—a fragile but increasingly disciplined alliance led by Naftali Bennett and Gadi Eisenkot’s Yashar party—is projected to reach 60 seats, just one shy of a governing majority. In a joint-run scenario, a Bennett-Eisenkot ticket is projected to command 33 seats, effectively shifting the mandate away from the Likud.

The strategic risk is compounded by the expansion of the Lebanon buffer zone. Netanyahu and Defense Minister Israel Katz have committed to a "security strip" extending to the Litani River, effectively occupying 8% of Lebanese territory. While framed as a defensive necessity to push Hezbollah’s anti-tank missiles out of range, military analysts warn of a "Gaza model" unfolding in the north. Over one million Lebanese have been displaced, and the systematic destruction of infrastructure in southern villages has drawn sharp rebukes from the EU and key G7 leaders. "We are seeing the creation of a humanitarian vacuum that Hezbollah will eventually fill with a new generation of recruits," warned one former Mossad official during an interview on Israeli national radio.

On social media, the analytical tension is palpable. On X, prominent defense analysts have pointed out that the strikes on Iranian naval assets in Bandar Anzali were specifically designed to disrupt Russian-Iranian cooperation, a move that directly complicates President Trump’s desire to stabilize regional trade. On Reddit’s r/politics, the discussion has shifted toward the inevitability of Netanyahu’s fall, with one widely-shared post noting that "Netanyahu is using a 48-hour window to try and rewrite the strategic map before the Trump peace plan freezes the board."

The Trump administration's response has been one of controlled frustration. The "Board of Peace," chaired by the President himself and bolstered by a $10 billion U.S. commitment, is moving forward with Phase Two of its plan: the withdrawal of Israeli troops to designated security lines in Gaza. Netanyahu’s 48-hour surge is a direct defiance of this "economy-first" approach championed by Jared Kushner and the President’s inner circle. By targeting Iranian industrial capacity, Netanyahu has effectively dared the Trump administration to impose a ceasefire on a region where the military facts on the ground are changing hourly.

The resignation of Ziv Agmon was the catalyst for a political collapse, but the strike surge is the symptom of a leadership that has lost its domestic mandate and is now seeking to maintain relevance through tactical escalation. As the 48-hour window closes, the question is no longer whether Netanyahu can survive the next poll, but how much of the regional order he will attempt to dismantle before the transition of power becomes unavoidable.
