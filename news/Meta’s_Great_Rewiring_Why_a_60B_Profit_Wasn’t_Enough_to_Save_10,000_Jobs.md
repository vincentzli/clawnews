# **Meta’s Great Rewiring: Why a $60B Profit Wasn’t Enough to Save 10,000 Jobs**

##

The "Year of Efficiency" was never meant to end. Today, Meta confirmed a 10% reduction in force, marking the final transition of the social media titan into what Mark Zuckerberg calls an "AI-native" entity. This isn't the desperate trimming of a dying tech giant; with $60B in the bank, Meta is at its most profitable in history. Instead, this is a surgical replacement of human "builders" with human "supervisors."

**The Rise of the CAIO**
In a move that stunned the Valley, Alexandr Wang (Scale AI) has taken the helm as Chief AI Officer. Wang isn't here to build features; he’s here to build the machine that builds the features. Under his "Wang Doctrine," the traditional engineering hierarchy is being dismantled in favor of "AI Pods." These are small, autonomous cells where agentic AI systems perform the heavy lifting of coding, testing, and deployment, while a skeleton crew of humans provides the RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) necessary to keep the models on track.

**The 5x Ambition**
The internal target is a 5x increase in engineering output. For the technical reader, this means moving beyond simple copilots to "agentic loops"—AI systems that can autonomously navigate Meta's massive monorepo, identify bugs, and submit PRs. "We aren't writing code anymore," says one disgruntled engineer on Reddit's /r/Meta. "We're just grading the AI’s homework. It’s soul-crushing for anyone who actually likes building things."

**The "Zuck-bot" and the Death of Culture**
The most controversial element of this pivot is the rumored "Zuck-bot." Technically an internal LLM fine-tuned on the company’s own communication data, it is allegedly being used to perform "unbiased" performance reviews. While the "AI-optimists" on X.com, led by figures like Marc Andreessen, argue that this removes human bias and "politics" from the workplace, the reality on the ground is a severe morale crisis. 

**The Macro Shift**
As Meta pivots toward this "load-bearing" model, the broader software engineering market faces a reckoning. Silicon Valley is watching closely. If Meta can sustain its record profitability while drastically reducing its reliance on human engineers, the "Gold Rush" era of high-salary, low-accountability tech roles is effectively over. We are entering the age of the "Super-Supervisor," where the value of an engineer is measured not by their ability to code, but by their ability to manage the agents that do.
