# The Swastika in the Sejm: Inside the Diplomatic Firestorm Redefining Poland-Israel Relations

The silence that fell over the Polish Sejm on the afternoon of April 14, 2026, was not one of respect, but of profound shock. As Konrad Berkowicz, a prominent figure of the far-right Confederation party, stood at the parliamentary rostrum, he didn’t just deliver a speech; he detonated a diplomatic device. In a meticulously timed provocation, Berkowicz unfurled a modified Israeli flag where the Star of David had been replaced by a black Nazi swastika. The visual, broadcast live across the nation, was accompanied by a rhetorical assault that labeled the State of Israel the "New Third Reich." The timing was calculated for maximum impact: today marks the annual March of the Living at Auschwitz-Birkenau, a day when thousands of Jews from across the globe walk the path of the Holocaust to remember the six million murdered. By evening, the "Berkowicz Incident" had eclipsed the solemnity of the commemorations, plunging Poland into its most volatile diplomatic crisis with Israel since the end of the Cold War.

The rhetoric was not an isolated outburst but the culmination of a two-year escalation within the Confederation party's ranks. "We are witnessing a genocide of exceptional cruelty in Gaza," Berkowicz shouted, ignoring the frantic gaveling of Deputy Speaker Włodzimierz Czarzasty. He specifically cited the reported use of white phosphorus munitions in civilian corridors, a charge that has been a centerpiece of far-right discourse in Europe throughout the 2025–2026 Gaza escalation. "You cannot shout 'Never Again' while you are doing 'Again' in the name of your own supremacy," he added, a line that was immediately amplified across X.com by his political ally and current MEP Grzegorz Braun.

The immediate reaction within the Sejm was one of chaos. Czarzasty immediately cut Berkowicz’s microphone and ordered his removal from the chamber, declaring that "displaying the swastika in the Polish Sejm is in no way justified by any political grievance." However, the damage was instantaneous. By 4:00 PM CET, the Israeli Embassy in Warsaw had issued a scathing statement, describing the display as an "antisemitic horror" and an "outrageous distortion of history." The Israeli Ministry of Foreign Affairs followed with a formal diplomatic protest, calling on the Polish government to strip Berkowicz of his immunity for criminal prosecution.

The legal weight behind these demands rests on Article 256 of the Polish Penal Code, which criminalizes the public promotion of Nazi or other totalitarian systems and the incitement of hatred. Under recent legislative amendments, Berkowicz faces up to three years in prison if convicted. The Prosecutor General’s office confirmed by late evening that it had already initiated a preliminary inquiry. This path is well-trodden for the Confederation party; Grzegorz Braun’s own immunity was stripped in 2024 following his fire-extinguisher attack on Hanukkah candles. Yet, analysts point out that Berkowicz’s move is more targeted, weaponizing the Holocaust’s most painful symbols against the survivors' descendants on their most sacred day of mourning.

To understand the analytical tension of this moment, one must look back to the April 2024 killing of Damian Soból, the Polish World Central Kitchen aid worker, by an Israeli airstrike. That event shifted the tectonic plates of Polish public opinion. At the time, Prime Minister Donald Tusk warned that Israel was putting "Polish solidarity to a hard test." The subsequent refusal of Israeli authorities throughout 2025 to cooperate fully with Polish prosecutors investigating Soból’s death left a vacuum of resentment. Sławomir Mentzen, the Confederation leader who spent much of 2025 distancing himself from Braun’s more erratic stunts during his presidential bid, has been forced into a corner. While Mentzen has avoided direct praise for the swastika display, his social media posts today have focused entirely on the "hypocrisy of international law," a calculated attempt to retain his nationalist base.

The social media storm has mirrored the fractured reality. On X.com, the hashtags #NewThirdReich and #Sejm reached the top of Polish trends. While mainstream commentators denounced the act, a vocal segment of the Confederation's digital base argued the symbol was "subversive art" intended to highlight military policy. One prominent nationalist account posted: "Berkowicz didn't bring the swastika to the Sejm; he just showed that the spirit of the 1940s has migrated." This type of "rhetorical laundering" has become a hallmark of European far-right politics in the mid-2020s.

International organizations have been swift. The Anti-Defamation League (ADL) released a briefing noting that comparing Israel to Nazi Germany is a "primary driver of contemporary antisemitism." In Washington, the response from the White House has been one of stern caution. President Donald Trump, currently serving his second term, has maintained a staunchly pro-Israel stance, but his "America First" doctrine has also encouraged NATO allies to handle internal dissent independently. However, State Department sources suggest that if Poland does not take "decisive legal action," it could complicate discussions regarding the permanent stationing of additional US troops.

As night falls on Warsaw, the political fallout is only beginning. The Sejm leadership is expected to meet tomorrow morning to vote on financial penalties, but the broader question remains: has the far-right successfully shifted the "Overton Window" of Polish discourse? By using the most taboo symbol in European history, Berkowicz has ensured that the conversation about Gaza in Poland is no longer about aid, but about the very legitimacy of the Israeli state. For a nation that houses the ruins of Auschwitz, the return of the swastika to the halls of power represents a psychic break that may take years to heal.
