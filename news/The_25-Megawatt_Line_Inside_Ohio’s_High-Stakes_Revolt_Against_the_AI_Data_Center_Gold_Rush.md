# The 25-Megawatt Line: Inside Ohio’s High-Stakes Revolt Against the AI Data Center Gold Rush

On a windswept Tuesday in early April 2026, the gravel parking lots of Miami County’s rural township halls became the front lines of a constitutional insurgency. Just days after the Ohio Ballot Board’s April 2 certification of a proposed constitutional amendment, volunteers for Ohio Residents for Responsible Development (ORRD) began the grueling march toward 413,487 signatures. Their goal is a regulatory "kill switch" for the tech industry’s most ambitious expansion: a ban on any new data center facility exceeding a 25-megawatt monthly load. In the world of "hyperscale" computing, where a single campus can draw 500 megawatts—enough to power a medium-sized city—the 25MW limit is an existential threat to the industry’s business model.

The conflict represents a fracture in the "Silicon Heartland" expansion championed by state officials. While the tech industry promises an "AI economy" windfall, residents in counties like Miami and Clermont describe a reality of non-disclosure agreements (NDAs), soaring utility bills, and a perceived assault on their natural resources. The tension is underscored by a jarring statistic: residential electricity rates in Ohio rose by 23.3% in July 2025. While tech giants like Amazon, Meta, and Google negotiate massive tax abatements, the average Ohioan is left holding the bill for the massive grid infrastructure required to support the cloud.

"We are being asked to subsidize our own displacement," says Austin Baurichter, a lead organizer for ORRD, during a signature drive in Piqua. His group is targeting the November 2026 ballot, leveraging a growing resentment over the "Project Shura" development—a 607-acre data center campus near Interstate 75 that remained shrouded in secrecy for months under strict NDAs. In Batavia and Miami Townships, residents have voiced similar outrage, claiming local officials were briefed in private while industrial parks were quietly rezoned for high-intensity computing.

The most explosive evidence of this tension lies in the Ohio EPA’s recent regulatory shift. In late 2025, the agency proposed General Permit OHD000001, a streamlined process for data center wastewater. The draft permit contained a line that has since become a rallying cry for the opposition: the assertion that a "lowering of water quality... is necessary to accommodate important social and economic development." This administrative stance has sparked a firestorm. Hyperscale sites can consume up to 5 million gallons of water daily—often pulled from the same buried valley aquifers that provide drinking water for millions.

Environmental advocates, including the Miami Conservancy District, have raised alarms that the EPA's permit fails to regulate, or even mention, PFAS—the "forever chemicals" often used as biocides and anti-corrosives in massive cooling towers. Because these facilities rely on evaporative cooling, the process effectively concentrates contaminants before discharging them as "blowdown" into local streams like the Great Miami River. At a recent Piqua City Commission meeting, one resident’s comment echoed widely on local social media: "They take our water, they boil it, they add chemicals, and they dump it back into our fishing holes, all so someone in California can generate a chatbot poem."

The tech industry is striking back through the Data Center Coalition, which argues that the 25MW ban would signal that Ohio is "closed for business," jeopardizing billions in GDP. They point to the Public Utilities Commission of Ohio (PUCO) decision in July 2025, which approved a "pay-to-play" tariff for AEP Ohio. This tariff requires data centers with loads over 25MW to pay for 85% of their projected energy use regardless of actual consumption—a move intended to protect residential ratepayers from infrastructure costs.

However, for many in Ohio's farm belt, the economic promises feel increasingly hollow. President Trump’s administration has pushed for an "AI energy dominance" strategy to maintain the global technological lead, yet the local cost of that victory is measured in the loss of prime Miami County acreage. The battle is no longer just about jobs versus the environment; it is about the very definition of a community’s sovereignty. As the July 1 signature deadline looms, the question for Ohioans is whether the digital future is worth the physical price of the land and water they leave behind.
