# The Data Caliphate: Inside the Leaked Palantir Manifesto and the “Colonization” of the NHS

On April 18, 2026, a 22-point document titled simply “The Technological Republic” was uploaded to Palantir’s official X account, then deleted within ninety minutes. By the time it vanished, it had already been archived by digital rights groups and rebranded by critics as the “Palantir Manifesto.” The document, a condensed ideological roadmap for CEO Alex Karp’s book of the same name, did more than outline a business strategy; it provided a window into what economist Yanis Varoufakis has termed “techno-feudalism,” sparking a political firestorm that now threatens the British government’s £480 million Federated Data Platform (FDP) contract.

The manifesto’s language is deliberately provocative, oscillating between geopolitical philosophy and what UK privacy advocate Martha Spurrier called “the rhetoric of a Bond villain.” Among the viral quotes is the assertion that “hard power in this century will be built on software,” and a chilling call to undo the “postwar neutering of Germany and Japan” to restore a software-driven Western hegemony. But it was a specific strategic directive within the document that ignited the London streets: an explicit plan to “colonize the British health data ecosystem” to provide the high-fidelity training data necessary for Palantir’s proprietary Artificial Intelligence Platform (AIP).

This “colonization” is not merely metaphorical. An investigation into the technical requirements of the FDP versus the surveillance capabilities described in the manifesto reveals a significant “capability gap.” While the official NHS contract frames the FDP as a tool for reducing waiting lists through better resource allocation, the manifesto describes it as a “digital nervous system” capable of monitoring staff performance and patient behavior in real-time. This aligns with the “staff access scandal” of April 2026, where it was revealed that Palantir engineers had been granted NHS.net email accounts and unfettered access to internal SharePoint systems—effectively integrating private contractors into the bedrock of the state.

The political fallout has been devastating for Health Secretary Wes Streeting. Leaked briefings from June 2025 show that Streeting was warned by civil servants that Palantir’s reputation was a “terminal barrier” to public trust, yet he continued to champion the deal. Now, with the manifesto in the public domain, the Labour government is facing a rebellion from its own backbenches. Conservative critics, meanwhile, have pivoted to a nationalist defense of data sovereignty, with some MPs accusing the government of “selling the crown jewels to a CIA-linked landlord.” In the United States, the Trump administration has remained notably silent, though the President’s long-standing ties to Palantir co-founder Peter Thiel—who famously described the NHS as “Stockholm Syndrome”—suggest a continued alignment between Silicon Valley’s “hard power” ambitions and the current White House.

The organized resistance has been led by Foxglove, a legal non-profit that has successfully leveraged FOI requests to expose the “Break Clause” in the FDP contract. According to internal NHS emails obtained by *The Guardian*, health bosses are terrified that the “negative sentiment” will lead to a mass patient opt-out, rendering the data platform useless. The BMA has already urged its members to limit their engagement with the FDP, citing a “clash of values” between the medical oath and a company that openly mocks “hollow pluralism.”

At the heart of the debate is the concept of “Data Feudalism.” In this model, the NHS is no longer an owner of its infrastructure but a tenant in Palantir’s “Foundry” ecosystem. By integrating the NHS’s operational logic into a proprietary “Ontology” system, Palantir creates a vendor lock-in so profound that the cost of exit becomes unthinkable. This is the “cloud rent” that Varoufakis warns about: the NHS provides the data (the labor), and Palantir owns the platform (the capital), extracting value from every patient interaction to train models it then sells back to the global market.

The manifesto’s rejection of “regressive cultures” and its advocacy for “Universal National Service” have added a layer of ideological toxicity to what was once a technical debate about database management. On Reddit’s r/politics, the discussion has shifted from data privacy to the survival of the democratic state. “This isn’t a software update,” one viral post noted, “it’s a coup by spreadsheet.”

As of April 21, 2026, the British government is reportedly weighing the activation of the contract’s break clause, which would allow an exit in early 2027. However, with Palantir’s software already deeply embedded in several NHS trusts, the “colonization” may already be too advanced to reverse without a total systemic collapse of the health service’s digital backbone.
