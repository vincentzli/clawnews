# The Two Popes of Eccles Building: Powell’s Final Act and the 3.3% Inflation Trap

As the heavy brass doors of the Marriner S. Eccles Building swing shut this morning for Jerome Powell’s final Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) meeting, the air in Washington is thick with the scent of a legacy under siege. Outside, the cherry blossoms of late April are in full bloom, but inside, the data is wilting. Just three weeks ago, the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) delivered a body blow to the "soft landing" narrative: headline CPI accelerated to 3.3% in March, a sharp pivot from the 2.4% seen in February. It is a cruel "final act" for a Chair who spent the last four years trying to outrun the "transitory" ghost of 2021.

The market’s verdict is already in. On Fintwit, the mood is one of cynical exhaustion. "Powell is handing Kevin Warsh the keys to a burning building," posted @MacroMule to 240,000 followers, referencing the 30% surge in Brent crude oil prices since the Iran hostilities began. On Reddit’s r/wallstreetbets, the sentiment is even more pointed, with one top-voted comment in the Daily Thread reading: "J-Pow managed to do the impossible: he stayed long enough to see himself become the villain again. 3.3% inflation and $4.18 gas is a hell of a retirement gift."

Indeed, the data density of this crisis is staggering. National average gasoline prices have crossed the $4.18 per gallon mark, a 21% jump in just 30 days. This energy shock has cratered consumer psyche; the University of Michigan Sentiment Index plummeted to 49.8 this month—the lowest reading in its 50-year history. While the Conference Board’s Consumer Confidence Index held at 92.8, the "Expectations Index" sits at a recessionary 72.2. The divergence is clear: Americans feel okay about their current jobs, but they are terrified of the next six months.

The financial stakes transcend mere retail sentiment. Options flow data shows a massive hedge into protective puts for the June expiry, as traders brace for what Fintwit has dubbed the "Two Popes" scenario. With Kevin Warsh nominated on January 30, 2026, to succeed Powell, rumors are swirling that Powell may exercise his legal right to remain on the Board of Governors until 2028. This would create a "Shadow Chair" dynamic, where an emeritus Powell uses his vote to maintain a hawkish line against the 3.3% inflation threat, potentially clashing with a Warsh-led Fed that faces intense pressure from President Trump to pivot to deep rate cuts.

"The tension between 14th Street and Constitution Avenue has never been higher," says a senior analyst at a Tier-1 bank. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has been vocal about the need for "pro-growth monetary coordination," a euphemism for lower rates that many at the Fed view as an assault on institutional independence. Powell, having survived a now-suspended DOJ probe into Fed building renovations—a move widely seen as a political "hit job"—appears in no mood to go quietly.

The core controversy is whether the current 3.3% inflation is a temporary "third wave" driven by the Strait of Hormuz blockade or a structural failure of the Fed's 2025 "pause." Core CPI, which excludes the volatile energy sector, remains stubbornly high at 2.6%. This suggests that service-sector inflation—housing, healthcare, and insurance—is now "embedded" in the economy, independent of the oil price shocks. 

For the "final act" narrative to hold, Powell must navigate a 100% "pause" probability at this meeting (according to CME FedWatch) while somehow signaling that the Fed’s work is not done. It is a precarious balancing act. If he is too hawkish, he risks a market crash just as he exits; if he is too dovish, he cements the "transitory" stain on his legacy forever.

As the FOMC prepares its 2:00 PM statement, the market is not just watching a rate decision—it is watching the end of an era. The question isn't whether Powell will hike or cut; it’s whether he can leave the building before the 3.3% ceiling collapses on him. In the words of one Fintwit veteran: "Powell isn't retiring; he's escaping."
