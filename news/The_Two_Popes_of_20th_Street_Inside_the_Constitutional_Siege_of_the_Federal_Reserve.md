# The Two Popes of 20th Street: Inside the Constitutional Siege of the Federal Reserve

On the morning of April 29, 2026, the quiet, marble-lined corridors of the Marriner S. Eccles Building felt less like the headquarters of global finance and more like a fortress under siege. Within an hour of each other, two seismic events reshaped the American institutional landscape. First, the Senate Banking Committee voted 13–11 along strict party lines to advance Kevin Warsh as the next Chair of the Federal Reserve. Then, Jerome Powell—the man the White House has spent sixteen months trying to humiliate into resignation—stepped to a podium to deliver a brief, icy statement: he is not leaving.

By announcing he will remain on the Fed’s Board of Governors until his term expires in January 2028, Powell has triggered what Wall Street is already calling the “Two Popes” crisis. For the first time in modern history, an outgoing Chair will remain in the building as a voting member, casting a long, shadow-like influence over his successor and defying a President who has characterized the central bank as an "enemy of the state."

The showdown is the culmination of a high-stakes campaign by the second Trump administration to achieve "regime change" at the Fed. The strategy, originally floated by Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent as the “shadow Fed chair” concept, sought to render Powell a lame duck by nominating his successor nearly six months early. The goal was to use market "forward guidance" against the Fed itself; if investors began pricing in the "Warsh pivot" toward lower rates, Powell’s current policy stance would become irrelevant.

However, the administration’s tactics extended far beyond economic theory. For the past year, the Department of Justice, led by U.S. Attorney for D.C. Jeanine Pirro, has pursued a criminal investigation into the $2.5 billion renovation of the Eccles Building. The probe focused on whether Powell misled Congress about "luxury features" in the 1930s-era headquarters—a project President Trump frequently mocked as a "Taj Mahal" during a construction site tour in July 2025.

"The threat of criminal charges," Powell said in a video statement earlier this year, "is a consequence of the Federal Reserve setting interest rates based on our best assessment of what will serve the public, rather than following the preferences of the President."

The legal siege only broke last week. On April 24, 2026, the DOJ announced it was closing the criminal investigation without charges, referring the matter to the Fed’s Office of Inspector General for administrative review. The move was a tactical retreat to clear the path for Warsh; Senator Thom Tillis (R-NC) had vowed to block any successor until the "bogus" investigation into Powell was dropped. With the threat of a perjury indictment removed, the Senate logic shifted, but Powell’s resolve only hardened.

Wall Street’s reaction to this institutional warfare has been one of "systemic volatility." In early January, news of the probe triggered a "Sell America" trade that saw a simultaneous plunge in stocks, bonds, and the dollar. While the S&P 500 has flirted with record highs, those gains are fragile. Headline inflation rose to 3.3% in March, driven by a 21% spike in fuel prices following the latest escalation in the Iran conflict. Investors are now caught in a pincer move: they crave the lower rates Warsh has hinted at, yet they fear the "term premium" associated with a central bank that has lost its independence to "fiscal dominance."

"The market is pricing in a regime change, but it’s also pricing in a constitutional crisis," noted one prominent financial analyst on X.com. "If you have two leaders with diametrically opposed views on the balance sheet sitting in the same room, the 'forward guidance' becomes white noise."

Kevin Warsh, a former Morgan Stanley executive and Fed governor, represents a sharp break. He has called for a significant reduction in the Fed’s $7 trillion balance sheet and a move away from the "data-dependent" approach the Trump administration blames for stifling growth. Warsh’s confirmation by the full Senate is expected before Powell’s chair term expires on May 15, but he will inherit a board where the "holdover" Powell still commands significant loyalty among the staff and several regional Fed presidents.

The White House has already signaled its displeasure. Secretary Bessent recently called Powell’s decision to stay a “violation of all Federal Reserve norms.” There is growing talk within the West Wing of attempting to fire Powell "for cause"—a move that would immediately go to the Supreme Court. The administration already tested these waters in late 2025 with an attempt to remove Governor Lisa Cook, a case that remains a flashpoint for legal scholars.

For now, the Fed exists in a state of suspended animation. On one side of the table sits Warsh, the hand-picked reformer promising to align the central bank with the President's economic agenda. On the other sits Powell, the institutionalist who has decided that the only way to save the Fed’s independence is to occupy it. As the May 15 deadline approaches, the global markets are left to wonder if the world’s most powerful financial institution can survive having two heads, or if the friction between them will finally spark the bonfire the administration has been stoking for years.
