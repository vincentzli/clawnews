# Double-Tap in Karaj: The Decapitation Campaign and the Strategic Erasure of the Islamic Republic

TEHRAN – As the 2026 Iran War enters its sixth week, the geopolitical landscape of the Persian Gulf has shifted from tactical skirmishes to a systematic "decapitation campaign." What began on February 28 as Operation Epic Fury has transitioned into a total war on the skeletal infrastructure of the regime. The events of April 2, 2026, have now been etched into the Iranian national consciousness as the "9/11 for Iran," a day when the surgical precision of Western airpower collided with the raw vulnerability of a nation’s pride.

The focal point of this symbolic and strategic devastation was the B1 Bridge in Karaj. Spanning the Shahid Soleimani Freeway, the B1—also known as the Bilqan Bridge—was more than an engineering marvel; at nearly 156 meters high, it was the tallest bridge in the Middle East and the crown jewel of the Northern Karaj Freeway project. On the afternoon of April 2, it became a mass casualty site. U.S. and Israeli F-35 Adir jets, supported by next-generation loitering munitions, executed a "double-tap" strike. The first missile shattered the central pylon mid-day. An hour later, as first responders and civilians celebrating the final day of Nowruz—Sizdah Be-dar—swarmed the site to assist the wounded, a second strike leveled the remaining spans. 

"The biggest bridge in Iran comes tumbling down," President Donald Trump posted on Truth Social shortly after the strike. "Much more to follow! We are making the world safe again from these radical actors."

The strategic logic behind the strike was cold. Pentagon officials identified the B1 Bridge as a critical supply artery used for transporting ballistic missiles and drone components from the industrial hubs of Karaj to northern launch sites. However, the human cost has been significant. The Iranian Health Ministry and the International Red Cross confirmed at least 8 deaths and 95 wounded in the Karaj strike, sparking a surge in nationalism that transcends traditional political divides. 

Simultaneously, the April 2 sorties delivered the finalizing blow to the Tehran Revolutionary Court. While the structure had been hit during the March 1 opening wave of the conflict, the latest strikes utilized specialized bunker-buster munitions to ensure the absolute neutralization of the judiciary’s central command. For the coalition, the court was a legitimate target—the nerve center of the regime’s internal security apparatus. For the public, its total erasure is the most visible sign yet that the 1979 order is being physically deleted.

The regional retaliation has been swift and asymmetric. Following the April 2 campaign, the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) launched a barrage of Fattah-2 hypersonic missiles targeting the Gulf’s economic lifelines. In Kuwait, the Doha and Az-Zour desalination plants were crippled, leaving 40% of the nation without a stable water supply. In the United Arab Emirates, the Ruwais refinery complex suffered extensive damage from drone swarms, sending global oil markets into a panic.

"If the Americans think they can cut off our breath by hitting our bridges, we will make sure the world dies of thirst," stated IRGC Brigadier General Alireza Tangsiri in a broadcast on state-affiliated Telegram channels. 

The economic fallout has reached a breaking point. With the Strait of Hormuz effectively blocked by Iranian sea mines and "swarm" patrols, global oil prices have surged past $120 a barrel. In the United States, gas prices have breached the $4.50 mark, testing the domestic political resolve for a war that President Trump continues to frame as a "final cleanup."

Inside the "hardened" government circles, the reported death of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei in early March has left the mantle to his son, Mojtaba Khamenei. Operating from undisclosed sites, Mojtaba has called for a "digital and physical insurgency," vowing that Tehran will become a "digital graveyard" for any invading force. 

On the ground in Karaj and Tehran, the humanitarian crisis is reaching a zenith. The destruction of the freeway network has stalled hospital shipments, and the "decapitation" of central government ministries has left local authorities struggling to manage basic utilities. As one prominent X user from Tehran recently noted: "The sky is never quiet. It is either the sound of the F-35 or the sound of the mourning. We are living through the end of a world."

The war, now entering its second month, has moved beyond a simple military engagement. It is a total war on the infrastructure of a civilization. As the "9/11 sentiment" takes hold in the streets of Iran, the risk of a generational hatred being forged in the rubble of the B1 Bridge looms larger than the tactical successes of Operation Epic Fury.
