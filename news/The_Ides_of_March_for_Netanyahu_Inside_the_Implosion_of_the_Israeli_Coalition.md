# The Ides of March for Netanyahu: Inside the Implosion of the Israeli Coalition

TEL AVIV — The silence inside the Prime Minister’s Office on 14 Kaplan Street is no longer the silence of strategic deliberation, but the heavy, airless quiet of a bunker awaiting a final impact. As the midnight deadline of March 31 approaches, Benjamin Netanyahu is not merely fighting a war in the skies over Tehran; he is fighting a war of attrition within his own hallway. The "Operation Epic Fury" campaign, launched in February 2026 alongside a resurgent Trump administration in Washington, was intended to be the definitive "total victory" that would cement Netanyahu’s legacy. Instead, it has become the catalyst for a domestic political implosion that forecasting markets now bet—with 50.5% certainty—will see him removed from power before the year is out.

The rapid deterioration of the Prime Minister’s position can be traced to a single, devastating 48-hour window. On March 25, Ziv Agmon, the Prime Minister’s acting chief of staff and spokesperson, was forced into a humiliating resignation. The trigger was a series of leaked private recordings and WhatsApp messages, first reported by Channel 12’s Amit Segal, that pierced the carefully curated "Mr. Security" facade. In the recordings, Agmon, a man once considered the gatekeeper of the Netanyahu inner circle, referred to the Likud’s own Mizrahi base and sitting MKs like Nissim Vaturi as "baboons." 

"A baboon is a monkey," Agmon was heard saying in one particularly toxic exchange. "It's not good that they opened Morocco to Israeli tourism; now we know where our Moroccans came from." The fallout was instantaneous. On X, MK Eliyahu Revivo, another target of Agmon’s vitriol, responded: "Agmon simply said out loud what he truly thinks in his heart. He is unfit to serve the public." But it was the messages regarding Netanyahu himself that signaled the true extent of the internal rot. Agmon reportedly described the Prime Minister as "old," "finished," and a man who "must go home," particularly in the wake of the strategic stagnation following the initial February strikes on Iranian infrastructure.

While the Agmon scandal eroded the coalition’s moral standing, the looming legislative guillotine of the 2026 national budget is what threatens its physical survival. Under Israeli Basic Law, if the 662-billion-shekel budget is not passed in its final reading by March 31, the 25th Knesset will automatically dissolve, triggering snap elections in June or July 2026. This would move the scheduled October vote forward by months—a timeline the Prime Minister is desperate to avoid as his Likud party currently trails in the polls, holding only 28 projected seats.

The budget is currently snagged on the twin shoals of Haredi conscription and war-related deficits. Finance Minister Bezalel Smotrich, facing a deficit ceiling that has been raised to 5.1% of GDP to fund the Iran campaign, issued a blunt ultimatum in late March: "If the Haredi parties are not willing to support the budget, then they should bring a law to dissolve the Knesset. If we don't want to vote on a budget, we will go to elections." The irony is a central theme of the unauthorized protests currently flooding Tel Aviv and Haifa; the very parties demanding roughly 6 billion shekels in "coalition funds" for religious institutions are the ones refusing to serve in the IDF as "Operation Epic Fury" enters its second month.

Strategic military objectives in Iran are now being held hostage by these domestic mechanics. A transitional caretaker government, which would take over the moment the budget fails, would be legally and politically hamstrung. It would lack the mandate to initiate the "Phase II" ground maneuvers some generals believe are necessary to dismantle hardened Iranian missile sites, yet it would be forced to maintain a wartime mobilization that is currently costing the Israeli economy over 1 billion shekels a day.

Forecasting markets like Polymarket have reacted sharply to the instability. Whale activity on the platform recently saw over $104,000 bet on a "Yes" outcome for Netanyahu’s resignation by March 31. Traders are also citing the sudden social media silence of Yair Netanyahu as a secondary indicator of a crisis within the PM's family. On Reddit’s r/Israel, the sentiment has shifted from wartime solidarity to political post-mortem. "The Haredi parties are squeezing the last drops of blood from a dying government while our soldiers are in the north," one viral post read. "Netanyahu isn't running the country anymore; he's running a clearance sale."

In Washington, the patience of the Trump administration—initially the Prime Minister’s most vocal cheerleader—is beginning to fray. While President Trump remains committed to the destruction of Iranian nuclear capabilities, the "Epic Fury" campaign has not yet yielded the rapid regime collapse promised in February. US officials have expressed private concern that a "leadership vacuum" in Jerusalem could leave the joint task force rudderless during a critical escalation window.

As the clock ticks toward the March 31 deadline, Netanyahu remains largely secluded. His schedule was scrubbed of public appearances following a televised address that sparked rumors of a health decline, with some market observers even questioning if the footage was AI-generated. He is a leader who has survived dozens of political deaths, but this time, the pressure is coming from within his own office and the cold math of the budget ledgers. If the budget fails on Tuesday, "Operation Epic Fury" will be remembered not as Netanyahu’s crowning achievement, but as the expensive overture to his final act.
