# The Apostle of Action: Inside Pete Hegseth’s Theological Overhaul of the Pentagon and the "Overwhelming Violence" of the New Chaplaincy

On the morning of March 25, 2026, the courtyard of the Pentagon—a space typically reserved for rigid military protocol—was transformed into a sanctuary of "overwhelming violence." Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, standing before a packed crowd of uniformed service members and civilian staff, delivered a sermon that effectively codified a new, more aggressive era of military chaplaincy. The centerpiece of the service was a prayer Hegseth read, one he noted was originally delivered by a military chaplain to the special operations troops who captured Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro on January 3, 2026.

"Give them wisdom in every decision, endurance for the trial ahead, unbreakable unity, and overwhelming violence of action against those who deserve no mercy," Hegseth intoned, his voice echoing through the PA system. He followed the prayer with a reading from Psalm 18:37: "I pursued my enemies and overtook them, and did not turn back till they were consumed."

This service was not merely a spiritual exercise; it was the public unveiling of a profound cultural shift at the Department of Defense. It came less than 24 hours after Hegseth issued the "Restoring the Divine Commission" directive on March 24, a sweeping policy change that stripped military chaplains of their visible rank insignia. Under the new rules, chaplains will no longer wear the silver bars of a captain or the eagles of a colonel on their work uniforms. Instead, they are restricted to displaying only the insignia of their religious affiliation—the cross, the tablet, or the crescent.

"A chaplain is first and foremost a chaplain and an officer second," Hegseth stated during the rollout. The rationale provided by the Pentagon is that removing rank makes chaplains more "approachable" to junior enlisted personnel. However, the move has ignited a firestorm within the ranks and among legal activists. Critics argue that by removing the rank that signifies their authority and accountability within the military chain of command, Hegseth is effectively transforming the Chaplain Corps into an independent ideological vanguard.

The March 24 directive also included a drastic reduction in recognized "religious affiliation codes," slashing the number from over 200 down to just 31. Hegseth dismissed the previous system as a "ballooned" bureaucratic mess that included "fringe and therapeutic belief systems" that had no place in a war-fighting organization. "We are making the Chaplain Corps great again by returning it to traditional ministry," he remarked, citing the need to move away from "therapeutic self-help" and back to "biblical foundations."

Hibbs’ focus on "national repentance" and "divine warfare" has been mirrored in Hegseth’s monthly Pentagon worship services, which have become a flashpoint for legal challenges.

"Secretary Hegseth is not just hosting services; he is using the machinery of the state to exert psychological pressure on federal employees to attend," Laser said in a press briefing on March 25. "When the head of the world’s most powerful military uses a religious service to pray for 'overwhelming violence' and 'no mercy,' he is signaling that religious conformity is now a prerequisite for military advancement."
