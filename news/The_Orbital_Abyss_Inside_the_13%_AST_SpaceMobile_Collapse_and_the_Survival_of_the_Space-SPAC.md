# The Orbital Abyss: Inside the 13% AST SpaceMobile Collapse and the Survival of the Space-SPAC

On Monday morning, April 20, 2026, the digital tickers on Wall Street carried a weightless velocity. Shares of AST SpaceMobile (NASDAQ: ASTS) opened with a staggering 13.4% gap down, sliding toward $72.36 as the reality of the previous night’s fire in the sky over Cape Canaveral settled in. The failure of BlueBird 7—the first of the company’s "Block 2" mass-produced satellites—to reach its operational 460-kilometer circular orbit has done more than just incinerate $30 million in hardware; it has ignited a fierce debate over whether the most celebrated survivor of the 2021 SPAC era has finally reached its "terminal velocity" on valuation.

The technical post-mortem is already clear. While Blue Origin’s New Glenn 3 rocket saw a nominal first-stage recovery, a failure in the upper stage left BlueBird 7 in a non-nominal low elliptical orbit of 154 x 494 kilometers. Without enough onboard propellant to circularize, the satellite is a $30 million paperweight destined to burn up in the atmosphere within weeks. While AST SpaceMobile confirmed the hardware is fully insured, the market is pricing in a far more expensive commodity: time.

"Blue Origin 'accidentally' deploys their competitors' satellites into the wrong orbit," one viral post on r/wallstreetbets quipped on Sunday night, capturing the retail fury that has seen the stock’s sentiment score plummet to a bearish 42. On Fintwit, the discourse was more clinical but equally savage. "At a $34 billion market cap, you aren't paying for 'insured satellites,'" wrote one prominent trader. "You're paying for a 2026 timeline that just hit a brick wall. The manufacturing is there, but the launch reliability isn't."

The carnage on the retail side is particularly acute. For two years, ASTS had been the darling of the "Space-SPAC" survivors, a title it earned by transitioning from a pre-revenue moonshot to a company with $70.9 million in 2025 revenue. But that revenue came at a cost. In FY2025, the company posted a net loss of $341.9 million against $406.7 million in capital expenditures. The April 20 sell-off is a violent re-evaluation of the "perfect execution" narrative that had pushed the stock to a 52-week high of $129.89.

The central question now is capital runway. Following a $1.075 billion convertible notes offering in February 2026, AST SpaceMobile entered the second quarter with a pro forma liquidity cushion of approximately $3.9 billion. Management has repeatedly stated this is enough to fund the first 100 satellites. However, the Blue Origin failure complicates the cadence. If AST is forced to pivot back to SpaceX—where Elon Musk’s Starlink is already operating over 650 direct-to-cell satellites—the scheduling conflicts and launch costs could erode that $3.9 billion faster than the company’s current $358.6 million annual operating burn suggests.

"The market was pricing ASTS as if it were a software utility, not a hardware company subject to the laws of orbital mechanics," says Andres Coello, an analyst at Scotiabank who has maintained a controversial "Underperform" rating and a $41.20 price target. Coello argues the 34x 2027 EV/Sales multiple is a "valuation bubble" that ignores the looming threat of the Amazon-Globalstar alliance and the sheer scale of the Starlink constellation.

The macro environment provides no safe harbor. In Washington, Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has signaled a tightening of the "Trump accounts" investment incentives, and the Federal Reserve is currently locked in a constitutional standoff. Jerome Powell, whose term as Chair expires on May 15, 2026, has refused to step down early to make way for Kevin Warsh, creating a "lame duck" paralysis that has kept the 10-year Treasury yield stubbornly elevated. In a world of 5% risk-free returns, the appetite for space-based speculative growth is thinning.

Retail "bulls" on Reddit’s r/ASTSpaceMobile remain defiant, pointing to the fact that BlueBirds 8 through 10 are already ready to ship from the Midland, Texas facility. "BB7 was a test of the New Glenn integration, not the satellite itself," argued one user. "The insurance check pays for the next launch. This is a gift for anyone who missed the move to $100."

Yet, the technicality of the "insurance check" misses the broader financial architecture. AST SpaceMobile’s backlog of $1.2 billion and its 2026 revenue guidance of $150 million to $200 million are predicated on having a 45-satellite constellation in orbit by year-end. With one launch per month now looking optimistic due to provider delays, the "Space-SPAC" survivors are facing a harsh reality: in space, as in finance, the most dangerous distance is the one between the launch pad and the intended orbit. 

The 13% drop today may just be the start of a multi-week de-leveraging as institutional investors, including Rakuten, whose founder Hiroshi Mikitani recently sold $271 million in shares, continue to look for the exits. For the retail traders of Wall Street Bets, the "carnage" is a reminder that in the high-stakes game of orbital infrastructure, there is no such thing as a "buy the dip" when the dip occurs at 17,000 miles per hour.
