# The New Space Race’s Gravity Problem: How a $1.2 Billion Deployment Failure Exposed the Fragility of Retail’s Favorite "Moonshot"

On Sunday, April 19, 2026, the promise of a global cellular broadband revolution met the cold reality of orbital mechanics and second-stage propulsion failure. Blue Origin’s New Glenn 3 mission, intended to be a triumphal entry into the heavy-lift commercial market, instead left AST SpaceMobile’s (ASTS) BlueBird 7 satellite stranded at a precarious 96-mile altitude—far below its operational requirement. By the time markets opened on Monday, April 20, the technical failure had transformed into a financial rout. Shares of AST SpaceMobile plunged as much as 17% in early trading, hitting a low of $72.75 against a Friday close of $85.53, as investors grappled with what many are calling "launch partner concentration risk."

The failure marks a watershed moment for a company that has become a darling of retail investors and a primary target for aggressive short-sellers. While the first-stage booster of the New Glenn rocket successfully landed on the drone ship *Jacklyn*, the upper stage’s BE-3U engine under-performed, failing to provide the necessary delta-v to reach the intended circular orbit. AST SpaceMobile confirmed early Monday that the satellite is "unusable" and will be de-orbited to burn up in the atmosphere. "Choosing launch providers making first-time launch achievements... is a choice," noted Reddit user univrsll on r/WallStreetBets, capturing the growing skepticism over AST’s decision to diversify away from SpaceX’s proven Falcon 9 fleet. "If it craters to ~$40 I might take a position on this absolute coin-flip of a company."

The financial stakes extend far beyond a single $110 million satellite. While ASTS management was quick to remind investors that the hardware is fully insured, the real cost lies in the "temporal dilution" of its 2026 roadmap. The FAA’s immediate grounding of the New Glenn rocket pending a mishap investigation throws the company’s goal of 45–60 satellites by year-end into severe doubt. This delay is oxygen for the short-selling community, which has seen its interest in the stock swell to 22% of the float. On X.com, traders pointed to the previous week’s regulatory filings showing insider selling by Rakuten founder Hiroshi Mikitani as a precursor to the volatility.

The macro environment provides little comfort. Under the Trump-Vance administration, Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has signaled a "maximum pressure" economic stance that has kept volatility high in capital-intensive sectors. With the Federal Reserve holding the funds rate steady in the 3.5% to 3.75% range, the cost of capital remains a primary concern for a company like ASTS that has yet to reach cash-flow positivity. "The irony is it’s the launch company’s fault... but ASTS suffers the consequences," observed Reddit user trackdaybruh.

Wall Street analysts are sharply divided, reflecting the binary nature of the space sector. Scotiabank maintained its "Sector Underperform" rating with a target of $41.20, citing the recurring risks of orbital deployment. Conversely, Deutsche Bank’s $117 target and Clear Street’s $115 target reflect a "buy the dip" mentality that remains prevalent among the company’s retail base. "Buying opportunity. It’s Blue Origin’s failure. ASTS will get punished in the short term, hence buying opportunity," argued WSB user doctorsidehustle.

However, the "dip" is increasingly expensive. Options flow on Monday morning showed a massive spike in May 1st $75 puts, as traders hedged against the possibility of the FAA investigation extending into the summer. The tension is palpable in the options market: "I am a girl who has just calls worth $35k and I am totally fucked expiration may 1st unless Jesus comes," posted user Briack89. The core controversy now centers on whether AST SpaceMobile can successfully execute its Block 2 constellation without relying on a launch partner that is currently grounded by federal regulators.

Economically, the failure highlights a "Space Race Risk" that many retail investors ignored during the 2025 bull run. With the Trump administration’s focus on deregulation, the FAA’s move to ground New Glenn is seen by some as a necessary safety check, but by others as a bottleneck to American orbital dominance. As Treasury Secretary Bessent navigates geopolitical tensions and their impact on inflation, the "space economy" is being forced to justify its high multiples in a high-interest-rate world. For ASTS, the BlueBird 7 failure isn't just a lost satellite; it is a test of whether a retail-led "moonshot" can survive the brutal vetting process of the actual moonshot industry.
