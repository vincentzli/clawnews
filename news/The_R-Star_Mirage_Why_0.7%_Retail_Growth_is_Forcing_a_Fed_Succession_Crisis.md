# The R-Star Mirage: Why 0.7% Retail Growth is Forcing a Fed Succession Crisis

On the morning of April 15, 2026, the Department of Commerce released a retail sales figure that sent a tremor through the fixed-income desks of lower Manhattan. A 0.7% month-over-month surge in retail spending for March—more than double the consensus estimate of 0.3%—has effectively vaporized the "soft landing" narrative that defined the first quarter of the year. Coupled with initial jobless claims holding steady at 212,000, the data paints a picture of a U.S. economy that is not just resilient, but potentially accelerating in the face of the highest interest rates in two decades.

The central mystery for economists in the spring of 2026 is why the consumer remains so indifferent to a Fed funds rate that has spent the better part of a year parked north of 5.25%. The answer, increasingly, is the "no landing" theory—a scenario where growth persists, inflation remains sticky, and the central bank finds itself fundamentally unable to cool the engine. This isn't just a statistical anomaly; it is a full-blown crisis of confidence for Jerome Powell’s Federal Reserve, whose term as Chair expires in less than thirty days, on May 15.

The tension on Fintwit has reached a fever pitch. "The Fed is officially pushed into a corner. You can’t cut into a 0.7% retail beat when core goods are reflating at a 3.1% clip," posted @MacroAlf, a prominent macro strategist, shortly after the print. On Reddit’s r/stocks, the sentiment is more cynical. One user in a 4,000-comment thread noted, "It’s not resilience; it’s a debt-fueled hallucination. My rent is up 12% in two years, but everyone I know is still buying $1,200 iPhones on 0% APR financing because they’ve given up on ever owning a house."

This "affordability fatigue" is the dark mirror to the headline data. While the Trump-Vance administration points to the 2025 "Budget Megabill" and massive deregulation as the engines of this growth, the cost of that growth is becoming undeniable. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has championed a "pro-growth, pro-tariff" agenda, but the 12-month lag on the administration's 2025 tariff regime is finally hitting retail shelves. According to a leaked Federal Reserve study from early April, tariffs now account for nearly the entire gap between actual inflation and the 2% target.

The analytical tension lies in the "R-star"—the theoretical neutral rate of interest. For years, the Fed operated on the assumption that R-star was low, perhaps 0.5% in real terms. But if a 5.25% nominal rate can't stop a 0.7% monthly jump in spending, it suggests the neutral rate has shifted significantly higher. This is the argument being pushed by Kevin Warsh, President Trump’s nominee to succeed Powell. Warsh has hinted that the "old Fed" was too slow to recognize the structural shift toward a higher-productivity, higher-inflation regime.

The data density of the March report reveals where the money is going. Non-store retailers (e-commerce) jumped 2.7%, while spending at "hobby, toy, and book stores" rose 1.4%, suggesting that discretionary spending is not just holding—it’s thriving. This occurs even as the "Iran Shock" of early April pushed Brent crude briefly above $90, though it has since retreated to $86.40 following a fragile ceasefire. "The American consumer has a higher pain threshold than any computer model can account for," noted a senior analyst at Goldman Sachs in a morning note. "The combination of the 2025 tax cuts and a 'low-hire, low-fire' labor market has created a floor for spending that the Fed’s traditional tools can’t reach."

But what is at stake? For investors, the "no landing" means the "Fed Put" is dead. The probability of a June rate cut, which stood at 64% in February, has plummeted to just 9% following the 212k jobless claims report. The 10-year Treasury yield has responded by clawing back toward 4.75%, its highest level since the "mini-panic" of late 2023.

The political dimension adds a layer of volatility that the markets are only beginning to price in. President Trump has been vocal on X.com about Powell’s "reluctance to get with the program," while Vice President Vance has spent the week framing the economic heat as a "triumph of American production over globalist stagnation." Yet, the divergence between the headline 0.7% beat and the 37% of Americans who tell YouGov they believe Vance has "little to no actual power" over the economy suggests a disconnect between policy and public perception.

If the consumer doesn't break, the Fed might have to. The "no landing" scenario is essentially a bet that the economy can outrun its own debt and the Fed’s interest rate treadmill. But with the May 15 leadership transition looming, the U.S. economy is currently a ship with two captains—one trying to throw the anchors and the other trying to fire the boosters—even as the passengers continue to spend like there's no tomorrow.
