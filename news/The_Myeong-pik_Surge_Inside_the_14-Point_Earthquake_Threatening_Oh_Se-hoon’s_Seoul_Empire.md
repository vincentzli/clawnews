# The Myeong-pik Surge: Inside the 14-Point Earthquake Threatening Oh Se-hoon’s Seoul Empire

In the fluorescent-lit corridors of Seoul’s City Hall, the administrative air has turned decidedly cold. For over a decade, Oh Se-hoon has served as the architect of the South Korean capital, framing his tenure around the "Global Seoul 2030" initiative. But as the June 3, 2026, mayoral election approaches, that framework is facing a structural crisis. A comprehensive poll released by Dong-A Ilbo on April 1, 2026, has revealed a 14.6-point lead for challenger Chong Won-o of the Democratic Party of Korea (DPK). The data—42.6% for Chong to 28.0% for Oh—represents a seismic shift in a race that was neck-and-neck just three months ago.

The epicenter of this shift is the "MZ Generation," the 20s and 30s cohort that proved decisive in the People Power Party’s (PPP) 2021 victory. Today, that support has collapsed under the weight of an acute housing crisis. By March 2026, mortgage rates in Seoul surpassed 7%, while homeownership among residents under 40 hit a record low of 17.9%. For this demographic, Oh’s focus on luxury high-rises and skyline deregulation has increasingly been viewed as a model that excludes the middle class. 

As one viral post on X.com summarized this week: "Global Seoul 2030 is just code for 2030-somethings not being able to afford a single room in Seoul. 7% interest isn't a mortgage; it's a life sentence." This economic anxiety has fueled the rise of Chong Won-o. Termed the "Myeong-pik"—a reference to the explicit endorsement of President Lee Jae-myung—Chong has transitioned from a three-term district chief of Seongdong-gu to a formidable mayoral frontrunner. His campaign is built on the "Seongsu Model," a technocratic approach that utilized co-prosperity agreements to mitigate gentrification and stabilize commercial rents.

The candidates offer fundamentally clashing visions for the capital’s future. Oh Se-hoon is doubling down on "Physical AI," proposing a "Physical AI Belt" from Yangjae to Suseo supported by a 700 billion KRW ($490 million) R&D budget. His "Rapid Integrated Planning" model aims to accelerate redevelopment projects from five years to two by removing bureaucratic hurdles. "We are in a supply-side emergency," Oh stated in a recent televised debate, attributing the housing shortage to President Lee Jae-myung’s central government regulations. In a bid for centrist appeal, Oh has even replaced the traditional PPP red with a lime green campaign uniform, signaling distance from a national party struggling in the political climate following former President Biden's departure and the return of Donald Trump to the White House.

Conversely, Chong Won-o is campaigning on a "Co-prosperity" model. His "Basic Housing" plan, which mirrors President Lee’s national "Basic Society" vision, proposes high-quality public housing units available for 30-year leases at 70-80% of market rates. While Oh dismisses these pledges as "fiction," prediction markets including Polymarket have moved aggressively, currently giving the DPK an 89% chance of victory in the capital. 

The international implications of this race are significant. Seoul remains the operational hub for the global High-Bandwidth Memory (HBM) and AI chip supply chain. Under the "Technology Prosperity Deal" negotiated between President Lee and U.S. President Donald Trump, the Seoul Mayor oversees the R&D and design clusters essential to the Gyeonggi Semiconductor Mega Cluster. Oh’s strategy focuses on attracting global tech giants through large-scale infrastructure, while Chong proposes four specialized "Smart Inclusive" zones in Yongsan, Hongneung, Yangjae, and Guro-Gasan to integrate AI into urban safety and welfare systems.

Regional diplomacy also hangs in the balance. Chong has advocated for "Beseto" (Beijing-Seoul-Tokyo) shuttle diplomacy to restore tech cooperation between the three capitals, a proposal PPP strategists warn could complicate the "economic security" alignment with the Trump administration. "A DPK win in Seoul would serve as a beachhead for a progressive resurgence in the 2027 presidential election," noted a senior political analyst.

As the campaign enters its final month, the PPP is looking for a "hidden conservative vote" in the affluent Gangnam districts. However, with the MZ generation increasingly favoring the "Myeong-pik" technocracy of Chong Won-o, the 14-point gap suggests a fundamental realignment of the Seoul electorate. The election has become a referendum on whether the city should prioritize global high-density growth or the social stability of its younger residents.
