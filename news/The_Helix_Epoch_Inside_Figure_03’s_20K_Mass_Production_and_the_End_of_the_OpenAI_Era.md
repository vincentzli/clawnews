**The Helix Pivot: System 2 Reasoning, System 1 Reflexes**
The technical heart of this shift is "Helix," Figure’s proprietary Vision-Language-Action (VLA) model. For years, the bottleneck in humanoid robotics was the "Sim-to-Real" gap and the brittle nature of C++ logic for edge cases. Helix solves this by unifying perception and control into a single transformer-based architecture. 

Helix operates on a dual-system logic: System 1 provides high-frequency (200Hz) motor control for reflexive balance, while System 2 handles 7B-parameter high-level reasoning. This allows Figure 03 to interpret a command like *"Tidy the nursery, but be careful with the glass"* without a single line of hardcoded "if-then" statements. By dropping the OpenAI partnership in favor of Helix, Figure has reduced latency by 40% and gained total control over the "tactile sensitivity" benchmarks. During our demo, Figure 03 successfully manipulated a 3-gram paperclip and a delicate wine glass—a feat previously reserved for multi-million dollar lab arms.

**Vulcan and the Soft-Goods Revolution**
On the hardware side, Figure 03 introduces the "Vulcan" resiliency policy. In the past, a blown actuator meant a $100,000 collapse. Vulcan allows Figure 03 to maintain bipedal stability even after losing three joints, essentially "limping" back to a charging mat rather than requiring a technician. 

Furthermore, the "soft-goods" textile covering isn't just an aesthetic choice; it’s a regulatory masterstroke. By encasing the robot in high-density foam and washable textiles, Figure has bypassed the "industrial cage" requirements that limit robots in domestic settings. This is a robot designed to bump into your grandmother and have both parties walk away unscathed.

**The $20,000 Disruption: Humanoid-as-a-Service (HaaS)**
The most jarring metric isn't the 3g sensitivity; it’s the $20,000 price point. At scale, Figure is targeting a manufacturing cost 90% lower than the Figure 02. For early enterprise adopters in California, the "Humanoid-as-a-Service" (HaaS) model is already live. 

"We are looking at an effective labor cost of $1 to $3 per hour," Brett Adcock noted. Compare that to the $20–$35/hour required for human labor in logistics and elder-care, and the math becomes predatory. 

**The Backlash: Unions vs. The "iPhone Moment"**
Not everyone is celebrating. On LinkedIn, labor advocates have labeled HaaS a "Trojan horse for mass displacement." The United Steelworkers (USW) recently signaled that "employment shocks" from AI-driven robotics will be the centerpiece of 2026 contract negotiations. 

Yet, Silicon Valley is doubling down. As one prominent VC put it: *"We are watching the 'iPhone moment' of physical labor. Figure isn't building a robot; they're building the first scalable OS for the physical world."*
