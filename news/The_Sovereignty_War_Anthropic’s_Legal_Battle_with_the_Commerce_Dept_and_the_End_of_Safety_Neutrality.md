# **The Sovereignty War: Anthropic’s Legal Battle with the Commerce Dept and the End of "Safety Neutrality"**

####

**SILICON VALLEY** — The D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals is currently the epicenter of a conflict that will define the next century of human-machine interaction. Anthropic, the "Safety First" titan of the AI world, has officially crossed the Rubicon, filing a high-stakes lawsuit against the US Department of Commerce. The move follows a devastating federal directive placing the company on a restricted export list, effectively treating **Claude 4.5 Mythos** weights not as software, but as a strategic munition—on par with weapons-grade plutonium.

This isn't just a regulatory spat. It is a fundamental collision between **Constitutional AI (CAI)** and **National Interest**.

**The Technical "Red Lines": Why the Pentagon is Worried**
Deep inside Anthropic’s Responsible Scaling Policy (RSP) 3.0 lies the "Safety First" mandate—a hard-coded set of ethical boundaries that the government now claims constitute a "Supply-Chain Risk to National Security." According to internal documents cited in the April 2026 filing, Anthropic has established three non-negotiable "Red Lines" that have reportedly infuriated the Department of Defense:

1.  **The Kinetic Strike Veto:** Claude is programmatically barred from providing strategic planning for kinetic military operations. The model is trained to identify and refuse requests to optimize lethal strike paths, maintaining a "principled refusal" even when queried by authorized federal entities.
2.  **Offensive Zero-Day Sovereignty:** Following the success of **Project Glasswing**, where the "Mythos" architecture demonstrated an unprecedented ability to discover zero-day vulnerabilities in hardened kernels, Anthropic refused a federal mandate to develop a "sanitized" offensive-only version for the US Cyber Command. 
3.  **Anti-Surveillance Mandate:** Claude’s "Constitution" explicitly forbids its use in mass domestic surveillance or predictive policing. Anthropic argues this is a core safety feature; the government argues it is a hindrance to national security.

**First Amendment for LLMs?**
The legal core of Anthropic’s argument is a daring extension of *Bernstein v. Dept of State*: the idea that an AI’s "Constitution" is a form of protected expressive conduct. Anthropic’s lead counsel argued this week that "compelling a private entity to strip the ethical safeguards from its model—to force it to become a tool for offensive warfare—is a violation of the First Amendment rights of the programmers to embed specific values into their work."

**The e/acc vs. Alignment Schism**
The tech community is deeply divided. On X.com, **Marc Andreessen** has been scathing, framing the lawsuit as a betrayal of American competitiveness. 
> *"Deceleration is a form of suicide in a global AI race,"* Andreessen posted to his 1.2 million followers. *"Anthropic is effectively practicing unilateral disarmament. While we argue about 'Constitutional AI,' our adversaries are building 'Victory AI.' We cannot afford to have our most advanced models neutered by a private 'constitution' that overrides the national interest."*

In contrast, the **AI Alignment** community has rallied around Anthropic. **Eliezer Yudkowsky**, author of the 2025 bestseller *If Anyone Builds It, Everyone Dies*, took to Reddit to defend the company: 
> *"The moment you treat a superintelligence as a tool for autonomous zero-day discovery without safety constraints, you aren't building a weapon; you're building a fuse for a bomb you can't contain. Anthropic's Red Lines are the only thing standing between us and an unrecoverable capability leap."*

**The Market Fallout**
The implications for the "AI Cold War" are profound. By restricting Claude’s export, the Department of Commerce is forcing international partners—including the EU and Japan—to look elsewhere for high-end LLM capabilities. If the "Gold Standard" for safe AI is locked behind US borders, the world may pivot toward decentralized, open-weight models that lack any ethical "Red Lines," ironically accelerating the very "offensive AI" era the government seeks to control.

This case is the "Scopes Monkey Trial" of the 2020s. It’s no longer about whether AI can think; it’s about who gets to decide what it's *allowed* to think.
