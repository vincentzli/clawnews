# **The 3.5% Rubicon: Kevin Warsh and the End of the Powell Era**

####

On the 10th floor of the Marriner S. Eccles Building, the air is thick with the scent of an ending. Jerome Powell, the man who steered the global economy through a once-in-a-century pandemic and its brutal inflationary aftermath, has only twelve days left on his term. But as he prepares to hand the gavel to Kevin Warsh on May 15, 2026, the data arriving on his desk suggests the "soft landing" he spent years engineering is curdling into something far more volatile.

The Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA) release on April 30 was a cold shower for a market already shivering. Real GDP for the first quarter of 2026 grew at a tepid annualized rate of 2.0%, missing the Wall Street consensus of 2.3%. While business investment surged 10.4%—a testament to the relentless capital expenditure in AI infrastructure—the American consumer is flagging. Real consumer spending decelerated to 1.6%, while imports surged 21.4%, widening the trade deficit and dragging on the headline growth number.

But it is the "stag" in the stagflationary narrative that has investors paralyzed. The Personal Consumption Expenditures (PCE) price index, the Fed’s preferred inflation gauge, jumped to a 4.5% annualized rate in Q1, up sharply from 2.9% in the previous quarter. Core PCE, which strips out food and energy, hit 4.3%. On a 12-month basis, headline PCE now sits at 3.5%—the highest level since mid-2023 and, crucially, the exact same level as the effective federal funds rate floor of 3.5%–3.75% that has held since late 2025.

For the first time in a generation, the real interest rate is effectively zero at the short end of the curve. The question haunting the C-suite and the Fintwit commentariat alike is simple: Can Kevin Warsh break the 3.5% floor without triggering an inflationary spiral?

"Trump clearly wanted a lackey who would lower interest rates when inappropriate, which could lead to another era of stagflation," wrote Reddit user **u/othelloinc** on r/investing this week. "It's basically Nixon all over again, just worse." This sentiment—that Warsh is a "sock puppet" for a White House desperate for easy money to service a national debt that officially surpassed 100% of GDP this year—is the primary headwind facing his confirmation. Senator Elizabeth Warren famously echoed this during the hearings, labeling him a potential proxy for political interests.

Yet the "Warsh Bear" case is more complex than simple political subservience. Warsh is attempting to resurrect the Greenspan playbook of the late 1990s. His core thesis, articulated during his April 29 Senate Banking Committee hearing, is that AI represents a massive, structural deflationary force. He argues that productivity gains are currently being masked by measurement errors and that the Fed has been "slow to cut," holding back an economy that could grow at 4% if unleashed from high borrowing costs.

"His jokey replies to Warren and Warnock at the hearing were a disrespect I have never seen a Fed Chair show in testimony," noted economist **Claudia Sahm** on X. This stylistic shift is part of a broader Warshian "regime change." He has signaled he will end the "dot plot" and forward guidance, preferring a meeting-by-meeting discretionary approach to avoid being "trapped" by previous commitments. To some, this is necessary agility; to others, it is the death of market predictability.

The market reality is currently being dictated by the Strait of Hormuz. With Brent crude trading at $102 a barrel and national gasoline prices averaging $4.30 a gallon due to the ongoing conflict in Iran, the "supply shock" is real and persistent. Ray Dalio has publicly warned Warsh against cutting rates in this environment, arguing that the U.S. is in a "stagflationary period" and that cutting prematurely would destroy the Fed’s remaining credibility.

Warsh’s defenders see a different path. "There’s an entire committee that votes," argued Reddit user **u/WFHaccount**. "Idk why everyone assumes this guy gets complete power... 12 people vote and he's one of them." This ignores the historical reality that a Fed Chair rarely loses a vote, and Warsh has already hinted at using "trimmed-mean PCE"—which currently reads a more palatable 2.3%—as a technical justification for policy shifts.

The stakes are measured in trillions. The Fed’s balance sheet remains bloated at $6.6 trillion, and Warsh has labeled this "fiscal policy in disguise." His plan to aggressively shrink the balance sheet while simultaneously pushing for a lower front-end rate is a "twist" that few in the market have modeled. If he succeeds, he proves that AI-driven productivity can offset a $100-a-barrel oil shock. If he fails, the 3.5% floor becomes a trapdoor into a decade of currency debasement and stagnant growth.

As Rick Rieder of BlackRock noted in a diplomatic nod to the man who took the job: "I congratulate Kevin on his nomination... but the 'This is not normal' theme is here to stay." On May 15, the "normal" era of Jerome Powell ends. The era of the "AI-productivity gamble" begins.
