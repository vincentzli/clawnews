# **The Twilight of the Sun King: Inside the Polling Collapse of Oh Se-hoon and the Rise of Seoul’s ‘Inclusive’ Challenger**

On the afternoon of April 20, 2026, the atmosphere inside the Seoul City Hall library is unusually somber. Outside, the early spring sun glints off the glass facade of a building that has served as the command center for Mayor Oh Se-hoon’s record-breaking four-term tenure. But with just 44 days remaining until the June 3 local elections, the "Sun King" of Seoul is facing a political eclipse that few predicted just eighteen months ago. 

The latest data from Metavoice, conducted for JTBC between April 11 and 12, reveals a staggering 16-percentage-point chasm between the incumbent and his challenger. Chong Won-o, the former three-term head of Seongdong District who secured the Democratic Party of Korea (DPK) nomination on April 9, has surged to 50% support, while Oh languishes at 34%. This follows a similarly devastating report from Dong-A Ilbo on April 1, which placed the gap at 14.6 points. For a politician who once dreamed of a 2027 presidential run, the numbers represent more than just a polling slump; they are an indictment of an entire administrative philosophy.

The collapse of Oh’s "Great Seoul" vision cannot be viewed in isolation from the national tremors that have rocked South Korea since the impeachment of former President Yoon Suk-yeol in late 2024. While President Donald Trump’s return to the White House has shifted the global geopolitical focus, Seoul’s local politics remain fixated on the debris of the People Power Party’s (PPP) domestic standing. Oh Se-hoon, once seen as the moderate face of the conservative establishment, has found himself tethered to a sinking brand.

Yet, Oh’s failures are also deeply personal and administrative. In December 2025, a major legal blow struck when prosecutors indicted the Mayor for alleged violations of the Political Funds Act, involving 33 million won in illegal polling payments during his 2021 by-election. On social media, the reaction was swift and merciless. "The Mayor who lectured us on 'Clean Seoul' was apparently buying his own approval," one viral post on X.com noted, reflecting a sentiment that has eroded the trust of the crucial "Generation MZ" voters who propelled him to victory in 2021 and 2022.

The physical landscape of Seoul has also become a liability. The "Great Sunset Han River Project," Oh’s 1-trillion-won signature initiative, is increasingly viewed through the lens of "vanity." The "Seoul Twin Eye," a spokeless Ferris wheel designed to be the world’s largest, has become a lightning rod for criticism as the city’s aging infrastructure struggles with climate-driven volatility. The derogatory nickname "Oseidon"—a portmanteau of Oh and Poseidon—resurfaced with a vengeance during the 2025 summer floods. While the Mayor pushed for high-rise redevelopments near the historic Jongmyo Shrine, leading to a bitter clash with Prime Minister Kim Min-seok, ordinary residents were more concerned with the repeated mechanical failures of the "Han River Bus," a water transit service that suffered operational suspensions within days of its early 2025 launch and became a focal point of National Assembly audits in October 2025 regarding battery safety.

"We don't need a Ferris wheel; we need sewers that work," wrote one user on r/korea, a sentiment echoed in the shifting demographics of the DPK’s surge. While Oh maintains a lead in the "Gangnam Belt" strongholds (44% to 24%), he has lost the narrative in the 40s and 50s demographic, where Chong Won-o’s "Seongdong Model" has gained legendary status.

Chong Won-o enters the race not as a firebrand, but as an administrative technocrat with a resume of "Smart Inclusive" success. In Seongdong, Chong became the first local leader in Korea to systematically tackle gentrification, creating "Sustainable Development Zones" and win-win agreements that stabilized rents in the now-trendy Seongsu-dong. His transformation of "Korea’s Brooklyn" from an aging industrial hub to a 500-firm social venture ecosystem provides a sharp contrast to Oh’s high-cost, tourism-focused projects. 

Chong’s policy platform for the mayoralty centers on "Urban Welfare" and housing regulations that prioritize stability over the speculative deregulation favored by the PPP. Where Oh warns that a Chong victory would be "Park Won-soon Season 2"—invoking the late liberal mayor’s legacy—Chong has countered by focusing on "life-friendly" technology. His IoT-based "Smart Shelters" and protection ordinances for "essential workers" have resonated with a public weary of grand architectural gestures.

The 20% of Seoul voters who remain undecided are now the ultimate prize. As of April 20, the PPP is in a state of visible "incumbency fatigue." Oh’s hesitation to even run for a fifth term, resolved only after a messy primary on April 18, has left his campaign looking reactive. In contrast, the DPK has unified behind Chong, framing the June 3 vote as a final judgment on the "Old Guard."
