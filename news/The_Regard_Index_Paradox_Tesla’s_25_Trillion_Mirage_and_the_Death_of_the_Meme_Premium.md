# The Regard Index Paradox: Tesla’s $25 Trillion Mirage and the Death of the Meme Premium

On the morning of April 12, 2026, the digital ticker for the "Regard Index" (RGRD)—a crowdsourced portfolio curated by the denizens of r/wallstreetbets—flashed a sobering crimson. After a blockbuster 2025 that saw the index post a staggering 76% return, the first quarter of 2026 has been a masterclass in mean reversion. YTD, the RGRD is down 4.5%, trailing a stuttering S&P 500 that has shed 2.1% under the weight of "higher-for-longer" terminal rates and the fiscal tightening of the second Trump administration. At the heart of this retail retreat lies a singular, polarizing titan: Tesla Inc. (TSLA).

The debate over Tesla has moved beyond the realm of traditional valuation and into a quasi-religious war between two irreconcilable data sets. On one side is the "$25 Trillion Moonshot," a figure Elon Musk crystallized in late 2025 during the unveiling of Master Plan 4.0. This valuation is predicated on the "Optimus" humanoid robot accounting for 80% of Tesla’s future worth. On the other is the "Falling Knife Reality": a company that, in Q1 2026, delivered just 358,023 vehicles against a production run of 408,386—leaving a record 50,363 units of unsold inventory rotting on tarmac lots.

"Tesla is a coin flip between the world's first $25T company and a $50 'fair value' car manufacturer," wrote one prominent user on WallStreetBets this week, echoing a sentiment that has turned the RGRD’s 9th largest holding into its primary source of volatility. The sub, once a bastion of "diamond-handed" optimism, is now fractured. Bears point to the collapse of operating margins to 5.8% in late 2025—a figure more in line with Ford or GM than a high-growth AI darling.

The macro headwinds are equally unforgiving. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, confirmed in January 2025, has overseen a shift toward aggressive fiscal discipline that has kept the 10-year Treasury yield hovering near 4.8%. Furthermore, the 145% tariffs on Chinese EV components enacted in April 2025 have acted as a double-edged sword: while protecting the domestic market, they have decimated Tesla’s supply chain efficiency, contributing to a -20% YTD slide in the stock price, which currently oscillates between $350 and $360.

Yet, for the retail crowd, the "math" for a $25 trillion valuation remains a seductive siren song. The thesis frequently posted on X.com involves a global fleet of 10 billion Optimus units. If Tesla captures 10% of that market with a $20,000 unit price and 30% net margins, the theoretical market cap dwarfs the current U.S. GDP. "We aren't buying a car company; we’re buying the labor of the future," noted a Fintwit influencer on April 10. "The Q1 delivery miss is just noise in the signal of the robotaxi rollout."

That "noise," however, is becoming deafening for institutional players. JPMorgan recently reiterated its "Sell" rating with a price target of $145, citing "free-cash-flow woes" and an inventory build that suggests Tesla has hit a demand ceiling in the premium EV segment. Conversely, Dan Ives of Wedbush remains the bull-in-chief, maintaining a $600 target and calling 2026 the "AI Chapter" that will see the Austin Unsupervised Robotaxi network expand from two to nine U.S. cities by June.

The RGRD’s other holdings tell a story of a retail market attempting to professionalize its speculation. While Reddit (RDDT) has cratered 40% YTD—weighed down by concerns over the monetization of its LLM data training—Micron (MU) has surged 24% on the back of the High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) cycle peak. This divergence suggests that the "Regard" investor is no longer a monolithic entity; they are increasingly splitting into "AI fundamentalists" and "Meme-stock holdouts."

The tension is most visible in the FSD (Full Self-Driving) debate. On April 11, social media was ablaze with rumors regarding the "Cybercab" design—a two-seater vehicle with no steering wheel, rumored to begin volume production this month. "If Cybercab doesn't ship by Q3, the $25T dream dies," posted a user on r/stocks. The market’s reaction to the upcoming April 22 earnings call will likely be the binary event of the year. Analysts expect an EPS of $0.34 on revenue of $24.2 billion, but the real number everyone is watching is the "inventory overhang."

As the sun sets on Jerome Powell’s tenure at the Federal Reserve this May, the "Regard Index" stands at a crossroads. The era of cheap money that fueled the initial Tesla surge is gone, replaced by a Bessent-led Treasury that prioritizes sound money and a Trump administration that views EV subsidies with skepticism. The shift toward long-term indexing within the WSB community—typified by the RGRD—is a parody of traditional finance that has accidentally become a mirror of it.

Whether Tesla is a $25 trillion AI behemoth or a $145 billion car company depends entirely on which data set you choose to believe: the speculative math of a robotic future or the cold, hard inventory of a saturated present. For now, the RGRD continues to bleed, a victim of its own polarized ambition. As one X user put it on April 12: "I didn't sell at $400 because I was promised the moon. Now I'm just holding a very expensive bag of robots."
