# The Sheraton Primary: Inside the "Big Three" Shadow War for 2028

###

The air inside the Sheraton New York Times Square last week was thick with more than just the usual fervor of the National Action Network’s annual convention. As the 35th anniversary of Rev. Al Sharpton’s organization drew to a close on April 11, 2026, the rhetoric on stage signaled the definitive end of the post-2024 mourning period. While the official theme was "Straightening Out Tricks in 2026," the subtext was a high-stakes audition for the 2028 Democratic nomination. With President Donald Trump’s second administration currently engaged in what speakers called an "all-out assault" on civil rights and DEI initiatives, the Democratic party’s "Big Three"—Kamala Harris, Gavin Newsom, and Josh Shapiro—have begun a maneuvering phase that is as disciplined as it is cutthroat.

The most jarring data point of the week did not come from the convention floor, but from a fresh Quinnipiac survey and internal Q1 fundraising ledgers currently circulating among the donor class. Pennsylvania Governor Josh Shapiro has quietly built a $10 million war chest in the first quarter of 2026 alone, a haul that dwarfs his regional peers. More critically for a party obsessed with "electability" after the 2024 collapse, Shapiro currently holds a decisive 10-point lead over Vice President JD Vance in hypothetical Pennsylvania matchups. Among independents—the demographic that ultimately handed the keys back to the Trump-Vance ticket—Shapiro leads Vance 58% to 33%. 

Shapiro’s "GSD" (Get Stuff Done) brand was on full display in New York. While other speakers leaned into the language of resistance, Shapiro leaned into the language of results, defending DEI not as a progressive checkbox but as "American values" tied to economic competitiveness. It is a centrist pragmatism that has seen his job approval in Pennsylvania soar to a record 60%, even as the national party continues to struggle with its identity.

However, the "legacy" wing of the party is far from ready to yield. Former Vice President Kamala Harris received the only true standing ovation of the convention, a visceral reminder of her enduring support among the party’s most loyal base. Since her 2024 loss, Harris has undergone a calculated rebrand. Her "KamalaHQ" digital operations have been folded into a sophisticated progressive hub, and her new memoir, *107 Days*, has served as a vehicle for a national "listening tour." When asked by Sharpton about 2028, Harris gave her most explicit signal to date: "If people want me to, then I will." Her argument remains anchored in experience; she speaks of "countless hours" in the Situation Room and being "a heartbeat away" from the presidency, a contrast she draws sharply against the governors currently vying for her space.

Meanwhile, California Governor Gavin Newsom is executing a "red state" offensive that feels more like a tactical insurgency than a traditional campaign. Newsom’s "Campaign for Democracy," seeded with $10 million in leftover funds, has spent the last six months targeting deep-red bastions like Nashville, Atlanta, and Rock Hill. His strategy is a curious hybrid of cultural pugilism and a plea for "cultural normalcy." On one hand, Newsom is the most vocal critic of the Trump administration’s rollbacks; on the other, he has spent his recent book tour for *Young Man in a Hurry* urging Democrats to move past "identity politics" to win back the working class. 

The primary field, however, is not limited to these three. Secondary contenders are already facing the "winnowing" effect of the 2026 midterms. Maryland Governor Wes Moore, once seen as a top-tier threat, has spent the spring navigating a series of bruising legislative setbacks in Annapolis. A mid-decade congressional redistricting effort he championed was blocked by his own party in the state senate, and he recently saw his vetoes on a Reparations Commission and climate funding overridden by a legislature that seems increasingly comfortable rebuffing his "action over studies" mantra. For Moore, the 2026 midterms represent a "must-win" moment to prove he can still command his home base before the primary season begins in earnest in 2027.

The social media landscape reflects this internal tension. On X, the debate over "establishment" versus "new blood" is reaching a fever pitch. As one prominent political analyst noted, "The Democratic party is currently a three-headed monster: Harris has the heart, Newsom has the microphone, and Shapiro has the math." On Reddit’s r/politics, the discussion has turned increasingly toward Shapiro’s PA numbers, with one user pointing out that "60% approval in a swing state during a Trump presidency isn't just a fluke; it's a roadmap."

As the 2026 midterm cycle enters its final, frantic stretch, the NAN convention made one thing clear: the 2028 primary has already started. The party is no longer looking for a defender of the old guard, but for a candidate who can survive the "winnowing" of a country that has fundamentally shifted its political center. For now, Shapiro’s $10 million and his 10-point lead in the Rust Belt suggest that the "GSD" lane might be the fastest one on the track.
