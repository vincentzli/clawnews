# **Bionic Civilization: How 1X NEO’s Tendon-Driven "Soft Robotics" is Redefining the Human-AI Interface**

Silicon Valley is currently divided by a fundamental engineering schism: **Rigid vs. Compliant.** While Tesla’s Optimus and Figure AI pursue a "hard" industrial architecture of gears and high-torque actuators, 1X Technologies is taking a radically different path. Backed by the heavy-hitting capital of OpenAI and NVIDIA, 1X is betting that the future of robotics isn't "Iron Man"—it’s biological.

The **1X NEO** represents the first legitimate attempt at a mass-market domestic humanoid. Its core innovation is a **Tendon-Driven architecture** that eschews traditional gearboxes for a system of high-torque motors and cable transmissions. This makes NEO "compliant"—physically capable of yielding to its environment. Weighing in at a mere **30kg (66 lbs)**, NEO is a lightweight compared to the 57kg Optimus. Wrapped in a 3D lattice polymer and a nylon suit, it’s designed to be hugged, not feared.

"Humanoid Robotics is a technology that opens a new horizon for physical AI," says 1X CEO **Bernt Øivind Børnich**. His vision is "Physical Intelligence" that learns by living among us. This is enabled by the **1X World Model (1XWM)**, a 14B parameter generative video model. As NVIDIA’s **Jim Fan** puts it, we are moving into the "Next Physical State Prediction" era. NEO doesn't just execute code; it "imagines" the completion of a task—like opening a door or folding a shirt—and then uses an Inverse Dynamics Model to translate those "mental" pixels into fluid motion.

The controversy, however, lies in **"Expert Mode."** To bypass the current limits of autonomy, 1X utilizes remote human operators in VR headsets to "embody" the robot during complex tasks. This has led to skepticism from tech figures like **Ryan Hoover**, who called the robot’s fluidity "uncanny." But as **Brett Adcock** of Figure AI notes, the humanoid form is an "unfair advantage" because it can ingest human video data. 1X is using teleoperation as a **Data Flywheel**, turning every "Expert" intervention into training tokens for their **Redwood AI** model.

Privacy remains the ultimate hurdle. The "stranger in the house" dilemma—where a remote operator could potentially access a private home’s visual data—is the primary friction point for domestic adoption. Yet, with a **Head Injury Criterion (HIC) < 250**, NEO is technically safer to have around a toddler than a heavy vacuum cleaner.

As **Karol Hausman** (CEO of Physical Intelligence) points out, the hardware bottleneck has been shattered. We are no longer building machines; we are training world models. NEO’s "soft" approach suggests that the first robot to truly live in our homes won't be an industrial worker, but a bionic companion that moves, learns, and yields just like we do.
