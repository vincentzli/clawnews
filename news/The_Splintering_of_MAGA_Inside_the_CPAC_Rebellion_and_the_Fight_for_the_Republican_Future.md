# The Splintering of MAGA: Inside the CPAC Rebellion and the Fight for the Republican Future

On Friday, March 27, 2026, inside a conference hall in Grapevine, Texas, American Conservative Union chairman Matt Schlapp attempted a routine crowd-work exercise. "How many of you would like to see impeachment hearings against President Trump?" he asked the audience at the Conservative Political Action Conference. Schlapp, expecting the traditionally pro-Trump audience to boo the idea, was instead met with enthusiastic cheers. Visibly taken aback, he shook his head. "No, that was the wrong answer," he corrected. When he repeated the prompt, the cheers returned. "Can someone bring some coffee out for the people of CPAC?" he joked, pivoting quickly to the necessity of maintaining the House majority.

The moment rapidly proliferated online. On X, *The Independent*'s Andrew Feinberg described the scripted stunt as "backfiring spectacularly" and going "horribly wrong." Across political forums like r/thebulwark and r/politics, users debated the underlying cause: was the audience simply dazed and confused, or was this a public fracture in the foundation of the MAGA movement? The reality, grounded in recent primary data and shifting 2028 betting markets, points to the latter. For the first time in nearly a decade, Donald Trump was absent from CPAC, leaving a void filled by mounting grassroots frustration over foreign entanglements, economic strain, and controversial executive actions.

The CPAC incident did not occur in a vacuum. It unfolded during the fifth week of "Operation Epic Fury," a major military conflict with Iran that began on February 28. Driven by Secretary of State Marco Rubio and Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, the campaign has pushed the U.S. military footprint near Iran to over 17,000 troops, including elements of the 82nd Airborne. While the administration concurrently pursues "Phase II" of a Gaza peace plan—pledging $10 billion to a reconstruction effort overseen by a Trump-chaired "Board of Peace"—the Iranian conflict has triggered severe domestic economic consequences. By late March, national gasoline prices approached nearly $4.00 per gallon.

Coupled with the economic anxiety is growing unease over executive decorum, recently highlighted by the U.S. Department of the Treasury's unprecedented March directive. To commemorate the July 4, 2026 Semiquincentennial, Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent announced that President Trump's signature will replace the Treasurer’s on new $100 banknotes beginning in June. This 165-year tradition-breaking move, alongside the approval of a 24-karat gold commemorative coin featuring the President's likeness, has drawn sharp criticism from fiscal conservatives who view it as a departure from institutional norms.

Data from the first quarter of 2026 quantifies this internal GOP rift. A March CBS News poll found that while 52% of Republican voters consider it "very important" for candidates to show explicit support for Trump—representing the "Legacy MAGA" faction—a significant counter-movement is crystallizing. A January Angus Reid Institute poll revealed a stark divide: while 93% of MAGA Republicans approve of the President, that number drops to 69% among the "Reformist" wing. This non-MAGA faction registers deep frustration with the cost of living (a net -30 score) and healthcare (net -20). Crucially, while Legacy voters heavily support the use of executive power to bypass Congress (+69), Reformists are almost evenly split (+2).

A recent Manhattan Institute study further segments the base, identifying "Core Republicans" at 65% and younger, anti-system "New Entrant Republicans" at 29%. Only 56% of these new entrants say they will "definitely" vote Republican in the 2026 midterms. This friction played out directly in the March 3 Texas primaries. While Reformist Rep. Dan Crenshaw was ousted by hardline Legacy challenger Steve Toth, incumbent Senator John Cornyn narrowly forced a May 26 runoff against Attorney General Ken Paxton, indicating the traditionalist wing retains substantial primary influence.

This volatility is rapidly reshaping the 2028 primary landscape. On prediction markets like Polymarket and Kalshi, Vice President J.D. Vance remains the frontrunner at approximately 36.5%, though his odds have declined from over 50% in late 2025. Conversely, Secretary of State Marco Rubio's stock has surged to 26.7% on Kalshi, buoyed by his commander-in-chief visibility during the Iran conflict. Meanwhile, former 2024 hopeful Florida Governor Ron DeSantis has faded into the low single digits, currently trading around 3.4% as his national momentum stalls.

The cheers in Grapevine were not an anomaly; they were an auditory manifestation of a coalition under severe stress. As the administration balances a multi-front Middle Eastern policy with domestic economic turbulence, the 2026 midterms loom not just as a referendum on the opposition, but as a battle for the very definition of modern conservatism.
